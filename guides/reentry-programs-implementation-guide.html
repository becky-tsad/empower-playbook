Executive Summary

Recidivism is fundamentally an economic problem. Within three years, 68% of released individuals return to incarceration—not because they want to reoffend, but because they face impossible barriers: no housing, no job, benefits gaps, and fractured family connections. Traditional re-entry programs address these issues in silos. The Empower Method changes that.

The Empower Method for Re-Entry creates pathways to economic stability starting before release, with continuous navigator support through the critical first year and beyond.

The Core Insight

The first 72 hours after release determine everything. Individuals who leave prison with housing secured, an employer waiting, and a navigator by their side have dramatically different outcomes than those who walk out with $50 and a bus ticket.

The Four Non-Negotiables for Re-Entry

1

Pre-Release Engagement (90-180 Days Before)

Begin relationship building and planning inside. Secure housing commitments, employer connections, and benefits applications before the release date—not after.

2

Re-Entry Navigator Model

One dedicated navigator per 20-25 participants. Same person from pre-release through 18+ months post-release. Trust-based relationship is the foundation of all success.

3

Day-One Connections

On release day: housing keys in hand, employer orientation scheduled within 72 hours, benefits applications submitted, navigator meeting within 24 hours. No gaps.

4

Benefits Cliff Navigation

Employment income triggers benefits loss. Navigate SNAP, Medicaid, and housing subsidy transitions so that working never makes someone worse off financially.

Critical Success Factor

Navigators should meet with participants weekly during the final 90 days. These aren't check-ins—they're working sessions where housing applications get completed, employer interviews happen via video, and benefits paperwork gets filed.

Re-Entry Navigator Model

Role Definition

Caseload: 20-25 participants per navigator

Duration: 180 days pre-release through 18+ months post-release

Relationship: Same navigator throughout—continuity is non-negotiable

Core Responsibilities

  • Weekly in-facility meetings during pre-release phase
  • Present at release or within 24 hours
  • Daily contact during first week post-release
  • Weekly meetings during first 90 days
  • Bi-weekly meetings months 4-12
  • Monthly meetings year 2 and beyond
  • Crisis response: available 24/7 during critical periods

Navigator Competencies

  • Lived experience preferred (not required)
  • Deep knowledge of benefits systems and cliff effects
  • Understanding of criminal justice system and supervision requirements
  • Trauma-informed approach
  • Strong employer relationship skills
  • Housing navigation expertise
  • Motivational interviewing certification
  • Crisis de-escalation training

Common Pitfall

High caseloads destroy programs. When navigators exceed 25 participants, they become case managers shuffling paper instead of relationship-builders creating change. Protect the caseload ratio fiercely.

Employment Pathway Development

Employment within 72 hours of release is the single strongest predictor of successful re-entry. But not just any job—participants need career pathways with advancement potential and benefits.

Employer Partner Development

Target Employer Characteristics

  • Ban-the-box policies: Delay background checks until conditional offer
  • Career ladder positions: Entry-level with advancement potential
  • Benefits eligible: Healthcare, retirement, PTO within 90 days
  • Schedule flexibility: Accommodate probation/parole requirements
  • Supportive supervision: Managers trained on re-entry challenges

High-Potential Industries

Industry Entry Wage Advancement Potential Benefits Access
Construction/Trades $18-25/hr Journeyman, Foreman, Contractor Union benefits common
Manufacturing $16-22/hr Lead, Supervisor, Quality Day-one benefits typical
Logistics/Warehousing $17-21/hr Lead, Supervisor, Driver Varies; larger firms better
Healthcare Support $15-20/hr CNA, MA, LPN pathway Strong benefits access
Culinary/Hospitality $14-18/hr Line cook, Sous chef, Chef Improving; varies widely

Employer Engagement Model

  1. Recruit: Identify HR leaders and hiring managers open to fair chance hiring
  2. Educate: Provide data on retention, tax credits, bonding programs
  3. Support: Navigator serves as single point of contact for employer concerns
  4. Recognize: Publicly celebrate employer partners; create competitive advantage
Employer Value Proposition

Fair chance employees have 13% lower turnover than general population. Work Opportunity Tax Credit provides $2,400-$9,600 per hire. Federal Bonding Program provides free fidelity bonds. Frame hiring as business decision, not charity.

Housing Placement Strategies

Homelessness is the fastest path back to incarceration. Housing must be secured before release—not after. A signed lease or confirmed bed is the minimum standard.

Housing Continuum

Housing Type Duration Best For Key Partners
Transitional Housing 30-90 days Immediate stabilization; no family housing available Re-entry housing providers, faith organizations
Sober Living 90 days - 1 year Participants with substance use history; structured environment Recovery housing networks
Family Reunification Permanent Stable family willing to receive; relationship repair needed Family counseling services
Rapid Re-Housing 3-24 months subsidy Employment secured; can sustain rent with cliff navigation CoC, homeless services
Permanent Supportive Permanent Chronic homelessness; significant disabilities Housing Authority, PSH providers
Market-Rate Rental Permanent Income sufficient; landlords willing to rent Fair chance landlord network

Landlord Partnership Development

  • Risk mitigation fund: Security deposit and damage guarantee pool
  • Navigator support guarantee: Single point of contact for landlord concerns
  • Tenant readiness certification: Pre-release housing education completed
  • Rapid response: Navigator intervention within 24 hours of any issue

Pre-Release Housing Checklist

  • Housing type identified and matched to participant needs
  • Application completed and approved
  • Lease or placement agreement signed
  • Move-in date confirmed (same day as release ideal)
  • Security deposit and first month secured
  • Utilities scheduled for activation
  • Basic furnishings arranged (bed, linens, kitchen basics)
  • Address registered with probation/parole
Housing Restriction Navigation

Many participants face housing restrictions based on offense type. Know your local public housing policies, registered offender residency requirements, and which private landlords have flexible screening criteria. Build relationships with landlords before you need them.

Benefits Enrollment & Cliff Navigation

Justice-involved individuals face a cruel paradox: they need benefits to stabilize, but employment income—essential for supervision compliance and long-term success—triggers benefits loss. Strategic cliff navigation makes work always pay.

Benefits Enrollment Timeline

Benefit Pre-Release Action Post-Release Action Cliff Risk
Medicaid Submit application 45 days pre-release Activate coverage on release date High: Income-based; varies by state
SNAP Submit application 30 days pre-release Interview immediately; expedited if eligible Medium: Gradual reduction with income
ACA Marketplace Research options if Medicaid-ineligible Enroll during SEP (release = qualifying event) Medium: Subsidy cliff at 400% FPL
SSI/SSDI Reinstatement application if prior recipient Expedited reinstatement process High: Complex work incentives
Housing Voucher Waitlist application (long lead time) Preference categories may apply Low: 30% income rent calculation

Benefits Cliff Scenarios

Scenario 1: Medicaid to Employer Coverage Transition

Participant earning $15/hr (31,200/year) in expansion state loses Medicaid at ~$20,000. Strategy: Time job start to maximize coverage overlap; enroll in employer coverage during open enrollment; use navigator to ensure no gap.

Scenario 2: SNAP Phase-Out

SNAP benefits reduce gradually but create effective marginal tax rates of 24% on top of regular taxes. Strategy: Map SNAP reduction against wage increases; identify income points where net income actually decreases; time raises strategically.

Scenario 3: Child Support Arrears

Back child support triggers wage garnishment of up to 65% of disposable income. Many participants owe tens of thousands. Strategy: Work with child support enforcement for realistic payment plans before employment starts; pursue arrears forgiveness programs.

The Cliff Navigation Conversation

Every participant needs a personalized benefits cliff map before employment begins. Show them exactly what happens to their total resources at $12/hr, $15/hr, $18/hr, and $22/hr. When people understand the math, they make better decisions—and they trust the navigator more.

Partner Integration Model

Memorandum of Understanding Requirements

  • Designated point of contact with direct phone/email
  • Referral response time commitment (48 hours maximum)
  • Data sharing agreement (with participant consent)
  • Participation in monthly coordination meetings
  • Outcome reporting to shared dashboard

Coordination Meeting Structure

  • Weekly: Navigator team case conferences (internal)
  • Bi-weekly: High-barrier participant staffings with key partners
  • Monthly: Full partner network meeting (all categories)
  • Quarterly: Outcome review and process improvement

Family Reconnection Protocol

Family support is the strongest protective factor—when relationships are healthy. Navigators must assess family dynamics carefully.

  1. Assess: Pre-release family interviews; identify support potential and risks
  2. Prepare: Family education on re-entry challenges; expectation setting
  3. Facilitate: Structured reunification conversations with navigator present
  4. Support: Ongoing family counseling referrals; parenting support
  5. Monitor: Watch for relationship strain; intervene early

Early Warning Indicators

  • Red flags: Missed navigator meetings (2+ consecutive), job loss, housing instability, positive drug test, new arrest
  • Yellow flags: Reduced contact, employment issues, family conflict, financial stress, supervision concerns
  • Response protocol: Red flags trigger 24-hour navigator outreach and partner notification; yellow flags trigger increased contact frequency

Re-Entry Funding Sources

  • Second Chance Act Grants: Federal DOJ funding for re-entry programs

  • State DOC Re-Entry Funds: Many states allocate re-entry support funding
  • Medicaid Administrative Claiming: Reimbursement for benefits enrollment assistance
  • Community Development Block Grant: Housing and employment services
  • Foundation Grants: Criminal justice reform, workforce development funders
  • Social Impact Bonds: Pay-for-success financing based on recidivism reduction
  • County/City Justice System Savings: Reinvestment of incarceration cost avoidance
ROI Calculation

Average cost of incarceration: $35,000-$45,000 per year. Program cost: $4,800 per participant per year. Preventing one re-incarceration pays for 7-9 participants. A 20% recidivism reduction in a 50-person cohort saves the system $350,000+ annually.
