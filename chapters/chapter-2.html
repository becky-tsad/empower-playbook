The Problem We're Solving

Before we can fix the system, we need to name what's broken. Three interconnected problems trap families in poverty—even when they're doing everything right.

Who We Serve: Understanding Stranded Talent

The people we serve aren't in crisis. They're stuck.

They're working—often multiple jobs. They're not unemployed. They're not homeless. By federal poverty measures, many aren't even "poor." But they can't get ahead. Year after year, they work hard with little wage growth, limited career mobility, and no clear path to stability.

Stranded Talent: A Better Framework

Stranded talent describes working people who are stuck rather than in crisis. They fall into three categories:

Persistently Low-Wage

Earning insufficient wages to meet basic needs despite full-time work. Working 40+ hours and still can't cover rent, childcare, and transportation.

Underemployed

Earning less than expected based on education or experience. The CNA who could be an RN. The factory worker with management potential. Skills and capability that go untapped.

Stalled

Little to no wage growth or advancement over time. Same job, same pay, year after year—despite showing up, working hard, and doing everything asked of them.

Research shows 36 million U.S. workers are stuck in high-churn, low-pay jobs. Source: Burning Glass Institute/Multiverse (2023)

ALICE: The Economic Reality

ALICE stands for Asset Limited, Income Constrained, Employed. It describes working families who earn above the federal poverty line but cannot afford basic household necessities.

The cruel irony: Because ALICE families earn above the federal poverty line, they don't qualify for most assistance programs—SNAP, housing vouchers, Medicaid. They're too "rich" for help but too poor to survive without it.

The Impossible Choices

ALICE families and stranded talent face daily decisions no one should have to make:

  • Car repair or rent? The car is how they get to work. The rent is how they keep a roof over their kids.
  • Quality childcare or utilities? Both are required for employment.
  • Fill the prescription or fill the tank? Health or transportation—pick one.
  • Take the overtime or see the kids? More hours means more money but less parenting.

These short-term decisions have long-term consequences. And they're not the result of bad choices—they're the result of a system that doesn't work for working families.

Why This Framing Matters

Positioning our work around unlocking stranded talent is more accurate—and more compelling—than a crisis-based narrative. These aren't people who need rescue. They're people who need a path forward. The Empower Method provides that path: a navigator who sees the whole picture, connects the dots, and helps families build toward stability—not just survive another month.

2.1 Fragmented Services = Fragmented Families

Meet Sarah. She's a single mother of two in rural Tennessee. She works 32 hours a week at the local grocery store—it's all her employer offers. She receives SNAP, Medicaid for her kids, and a childcare subsidy. She's on a housing waitlist. She wants to work more, earn more, and get off benefits.

Here's her current reality:

  • She works with a SNAP caseworker at DHS—sees them every 6 months
  • She has a TennCare eligibility worker—different person, different office
  • Her childcare subsidy goes through a separate state agency
  • She's on a housing waitlist managed by a local housing authority
  • She attended a workforce center workshop once, but never went back

None of these agencies talk to each other. Sarah tells her story five times. Each agency sees one slice of her life. No one sees the whole picture. No one is responsible for her outcome.

The Referral Treadmill

In fragmented systems, "helping" means referring. The workforce center refers Sarah to a job training program. The training program refers her to an employer. The employer needs childcare documentation, so they refer her back to DHS. DHS needs income verification, so they refer her to the employer.

Sarah runs in circles. She attends meetings. She fills out forms. She waits on hold. She's "being served" by multiple agencies—and going nowhere.

Every agency measures their referrals. No one measures whether Sarah actually got a job, kept it, and stayed off benefits.

Duplicate Programs, Competing for the Same Families

In many communities, three different organizations run job readiness workshops. Two agencies provide financial literacy classes. Four groups offer food assistance. They compete for the same grant dollars, serve the same families, and duplicate each other's efforts—while critical gaps go unaddressed.

Meanwhile, no one provides benefits cliff navigation. No one coordinates healthcare transitions. No one stays with a family for 18 months.

The problem isn't too few services. It's no coordination.

2.2 The Benefits Cliff Crisis

This is the hidden trap that keeps working families poor.

The Scale of the Benefits Cliff Crisis

3M+

households face benefits cliffs if income increases

51%

median effective marginal tax rate for families with children above poverty

95%+

marginal tax rate possible when Medicaid is lost

For many families, earning more means keeping less—the system creates invisible walls to advancement.

How a $1 Raise Can Cost a Family $5,000

Sarah's employer offers her a promotion: 40 hours a week, $2 more per hour. That's $4,160 more per year. She should take it, right?

Wrong.

That raise pushes her income above the childcare subsidy threshold. She loses $6,800 per year in childcare assistance. She's now $2,640 poorer than before the raise.

If she earns a bit more, she loses SNAP. A bit more after that, her kids lose TennCare. Each "cliff" punishes her for earning more.

The Poverty Trap: Sarah needs to nearly double her income (from $26K to $54K+) just to get back to the same standard of living she had before the raise. This $19,000+ gap is why families stay stuck.

Research Finding: Childcare Has the Steepest Cliffs

Childcare subsidies produce the steepest cliff effects of any benefit program. Families can lose $5,991 to $6,000+ annually at cliff points—often more than the income gain that triggered the loss. This is why benefits cliff navigation should start with childcare mapping for families receiving subsidies.

69%

This is not an unemployment problem. These aren't people who won't work. They're working—often multiple jobs—and still can't get ahead because the system punishes every step forward.

What Families Actually Need

Sarah doesn't need another job skills workshop. She needs someone to sit down with her and map out:

  • Exactly where her benefits cliffs are
  • How much she needs to earn to clear each cliff
  • A sequenced plan to navigate the transition
  • An employer who understands the plan and won't accidentally push her over a cliff with a well-meaning raise

That's benefits cliff navigation. It's the difference between telling Sarah "get a better job" and actually helping her get there.

2.3 Poverty Management vs. Poverty Alleviation

Here's the uncomfortable truth about most poverty programs:

They're designed to help people cope with poverty, not exit it.

The Difference

Poverty Management

  • ✗ Measures activities (referrals made)
  • ✗ 3-6 month program cycles
  • ✗ Success = enrolled in services
  • ✗ Rotating caseloads
  • ✗ Siloed services
  • ✗ Reactive (respond to crises)

Poverty Alleviation

  • ✓ Measures outcomes (income gained)
  • ✓ 18-24 month commitments
  • ✓ Success = self-sufficiency achieved
  • ✓ One navigator, full journey
  • ✓ Coordinated services
  • ✓ Proactive (prevent cliffs before they happen)

The Self-Sufficiency Standard: ALICE

Most programs use the Federal Poverty Level (FPL) as their benchmark. If a family earns above FPL, they're "not poor" by official measures.

But FPL is absurdly low. In 2024, FPL for a family of three is $24,860. No one believes a family of three can be self-sufficient on $25,000 a year.

That's why we use the ALICE threshold: Asset Limited, Income Constrained, Employed. ALICE households earn too much to qualify for poverty programs but too little to afford basic needs: housing, childcare, food, transportation, healthcare.

The Empower Method targets 225% of FPL—roughly where families cross from "surviving" to "stable." That's the real finish line.

Key Takeaway

The current system creates three traps:

  1. 1. Fragmentation: No one owns the outcome
  2. 2. Benefits cliffs: Working harder makes you poorer
  3. 3. Wrong metrics: Programs measure activity, not exit

The Empower Method solves all three—and Chapter 3 proves it works.
