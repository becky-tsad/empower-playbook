The Problem We're Solving

The system punishes effort. Three interconnected traps keep families in poverty—not because they aren't working hard enough, but because the system is designed to hold them in place.

Who We Serve: Stranded Talent

The families we serve aren't in crisis. They're stuck.

They work. Often multiple jobs. They're not unemployed. They're not homeless. By federal poverty measures, many aren't even "poor." But they can't get ahead. Year after year, they show up, put in the hours, and have nothing to show for it. No wage growth. No career path. No way out.

Stranded Talent: A Better Framework

Stranded talent describes working families who are stuck—not broken. They fall into three categories:

Persistently Low-Wage

Full-time work that doesn't pay the bills. 40+ hours a week and still can't cover rent, childcare, and transportation.

Underemployed

Earning far less than their skills warrant. The CNA who could be an RN. The factory worker with management potential. Talent that goes untapped.

Stalled

Zero wage growth. Zero advancement. Same job, same pay, year after year—despite showing up and doing everything asked of them.

36 million U.S. workers are stuck in high-churn, low-pay jobs. Source: Burning Glass Institute/Multiverse (2023)

ALICE: The Economic Reality

ALICE stands for Asset Limited, Income Constrained, Employed. These are working families who earn above the federal poverty line but can't afford the basics: housing, food, childcare, transportation.

The cruel irony: ALICE families earn too much for help—no SNAP, no housing vouchers, no Medicaid. But too little to survive without it. The system says they're fine. They're not.

The Impossible Choices

Every day, these families make decisions no one should have to make:

  • Car repair or rent? The car is how they get to work. The rent is how they keep a roof over their kids.
  • Quality childcare or utilities? Both are required for employment.
  • Fill the prescription or fill the tank? Health or transportation—pick one.
  • Take the overtime or see the kids? More hours means more money but less parenting.

These aren't bad choices. They're impossible ones—forced by a system that doesn't work for working families.

Why This Framing Matters

We don't do crisis rescue. We unlock stranded talent. These are families who need a path forward, not a handout. The Empower Method provides that path: one navigator who sees the whole picture, connects the dots, and walks with families toward livable-wage careers—not just another month of survival.

2.1 Fragmented Services = Fragmented Families

Meet Sarah. Single mom. Two kids. Rural Tennessee. She works 32 hours a week at the local grocery store—that's all they offer. She gets SNAP, Medicaid for her kids, and a childcare subsidy. She's on a housing waitlist. She wants to work more, earn more, and get off benefits.

Here's what that actually looks like:

  • She works with a SNAP caseworker at DHS—sees them every 6 months
  • She has a TennCare eligibility worker—different person, different office
  • Her childcare subsidy goes through a separate state agency
  • She's on a housing waitlist managed by a local housing authority
  • She attended a workforce center workshop once, but never went back

None of these agencies talk to each other. Sarah tells her story five times to five different people. Each one sees a sliver of her life. No one sees the whole picture. No one owns her outcome.

The Referral Treadmill

In fragmented systems, "helping" means referring. The workforce center refers Sarah to a job training program. The training program refers her to an employer. The employer needs childcare documentation—back to DHS. DHS needs income verification—back to the employer.

Sarah runs in circles. Meetings. Forms. Hold music. She's "being served" by five agencies and going absolutely nowhere.

Every agency counts their referrals. No one counts whether Sarah actually got a livable-wage career, kept it, and stayed off benefits.

Duplicate Programs, Competing for the Same Families

Three organizations run job readiness workshops. Two agencies teach financial literacy. Four groups hand out food. They compete for the same grant dollars, serve the same families, and duplicate each other's work—while the real gaps go untouched.

Nobody navigates benefits cliffs. Nobody coordinates healthcare transitions. Nobody walks with a family for 18 months.

The problem isn't too few services. It's zero coordination.

2.2 The Benefits Cliff Crisis

This is the trap nobody talks about. The one that keeps working families poor on purpose.

The Scale of the Benefits Cliff Crisis

3M+

households face benefits cliffs if income increases

51%

median effective marginal tax rate for families with children above poverty

95%+

marginal tax rate possible when Medicaid is lost

Earn more, keep less. The system punishes effort with invisible walls that block every step forward.

How a $1 Raise Can Cost a Family $5,000

Sarah's employer offers her a promotion: 40 hours a week, $2 more per hour. That's $4,160 more per year. She should take it.

She can't.

That raise pushes her over the childcare subsidy threshold. She loses $6,800 per year in childcare assistance. Net result: she's $2,640 poorer than before the "raise."

Earn a bit more, she loses SNAP. A bit more, her kids lose TennCare. Every cliff punishes her for working harder.

Research Finding: Childcare Has the Steepest Cliffs

Childcare subsidies create the steepest cliff of any benefit program. Families lose $5,991 to $6,000+ annually at cliff points—often more than the raise that triggered the loss. This is why navigators start with childcare mapping first.

What Families Actually Need

Sarah doesn't need another resume workshop. She needs a navigator who sits down with her and maps out:

  • Exactly where her benefits cliffs are
  • How much she needs to earn to clear each one
  • A sequenced plan to cross the gap safely
  • An employer who understands the plan and won't accidentally push her off a cliff with a well-meaning raise

That's benefits cliff navigation. Not "get a better job." An actual path to get there.

2.3 Poverty Management vs. Poverty Alleviation

Here's the uncomfortable truth:

Most poverty programs are designed to manage poverty, not end it.

The Difference

Poverty Management

  • ✗ Measures activities (referrals made)
  • ✗ 3-6 month program cycles
  • ✗ Success = enrolled in services
  • ✗ Rotating caseloads
  • ✗ Siloed services
  • ✗ Reactive (respond to crises)

Poverty Alleviation

  • ✓ Measures outcomes (income gained)
  • ✓ 18-24 month commitments
  • ✓ Success = self-sufficiency achieved
  • ✓ One navigator, full journey
  • ✓ Coordinated services
  • ✓ Proactive (prevent cliffs before they happen)

The Self-Sufficiency Standard: ALICE

Most programs use the Federal Poverty Level (FPL) as their benchmark. Earn above it and you're "not poor" by official measures.

That benchmark is a joke. In 2024, FPL for a family of three is $24,860. Nobody survives on $25,000 a year. Nobody.

We use the ALICE threshold instead. ALICE families earn too much for poverty programs but too little for the basics: housing, childcare, food, transportation, healthcare.

The Empower Method targets 225% of FPL—the line where families cross from surviving to stable. That's the real finish line.

Key Takeaway

The system traps families in three ways:

  1. 1. Fragmentation: Nobody owns the outcome
  2. 2. Benefits cliffs: Hard work gets punished
  3. 3. Wrong metrics: Programs count activities, not exits from poverty

The Empower Method solves all three. Chapter 3 has the proof.
