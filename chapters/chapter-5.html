Adaptable Components

What you can customize vs. what must stay constant

The Core Principle

The Four Non-Negotiables are constant. Everything else can be adapted to your community's context, existing systems, and resources. This chapter clarifies what must remain fixed for outcomes to hold—and where you have flexibility to make the model your own.

Why This Distinction Matters

Every community that implements the Empower model will face pressure to "customize" it. Funders will want their priorities emphasized. Partners will suggest shortcuts. Staff will propose workarounds. Some of these adaptations are healthy—they help the model fit local context. Others will undermine the very outcomes you're trying to achieve.

Knowing the difference is critical. We've seen well-intentioned modifications destroy program effectiveness in other regions. This chapter gives you clear guidance on where you have flexibility and where you don't.

What the Research Shows

Implementation science draws a critical distinction between core components (the active ingredients that produce outcomes) and adaptable components (delivery mechanisms that can change based on context). Programs that clearly define and protect their core components scale faster and maintain effectiveness across diverse settings.

Warning: Research shows that 52% of nonprofits modify their programs to fit available funding—often unknowingly compromising core components. This "funding-driven drift" is one of the primary causes of program failure during scale-up.

The Non-Negotiables (Fixed)

A quick reminder from Chapter 4: these are the four elements that must remain intact for the model to work. They're worth repeating here because as you adapt the Empower Method to your context, these are the lines you cannot cross. Compromising on any one of them will significantly reduce outcomes:

1. Benefits Cliff Navigation

Every family must receive personalized benefits cliff modeling before making employment decisions. This cannot be delegated to self-service tools or generalized advice.

2. Navigator Model (1:25 ratio max)

Navigators must have caseloads small enough for relationship-based support. Higher ratios = transactional interactions = lost outcomes.

3. Wraparound Coordination

Every service must connect to the goal: getting participants into livable wage jobs. Navigators coordinate across systems to remove barriers to employment.

4. Data Dashboard Accountability

Real-time outcome tracking must be visible to all stakeholders. No hiding poor results behind quarterly reports.

  • ALICE threshold (up to 225% FPL)
  • TANF-eligible families
  • Specific income bands (150-300% FPL)

Population-Based Targeting

  • Parents with children under 18
  • Specific employer workforces
  • Geographic zones (specific counties)

System-Based Entry Points

  • TANF recipients nearing time limits
  • Workforce program completers
  • Re-entry population

Employer-Based Targeting

  • Employees of certified employers
  • Industry-specific (healthcare, manufacturing)
  • New hires in first 90 days

3 Service Delivery Channels

How families access navigation services can vary by context:

  • In-person at central location: Traditional model with dedicated Empower office
  • Mobile/itinerant navigators: Better for very rural/dispersed populations
  • Employer site-based: Navigators hold hours at certified employer locations
  • Hybrid digital: Initial screening online, intensive support in-person
  • Community anchor-based: Co-located with churches, libraries, or community centers

Decision Framework: When to Adapt

Use this framework when stakeholders propose modifications:

✓

Adapt if the change...

  • Improves fit with local systems without compromising core functions
  • Addresses a real barrier families face in your community
  • Leverages existing resources more efficiently
  • Makes the model more sustainable long-term
✗

Resist if the change...

  • Increases caseload ratios beyond 1:25
  • Removes benefits cliff analysis from the process
  • Shifts navigators to transactional rather than relational support
  • Reduces data transparency or accountability
  • Prioritizes funder preferences over family outcomes
"The art of implementation is knowing where to be rigid and where to be flexible. Get it backwards and you either break the model or fail to fit the community."

Adaptation Examples

Here's how different communities have adapted the model while keeping the core intact:

Rural Appalachian Region

Adaptation: Added mobile navigator van for communities without public transportation. Navigators travel to families rather than expecting families to come to central office.

Why it works: Addresses real access barrier without changing the navigator relationship model.

Recovery-Focused Implementation

Adaptation: Integrated peer recovery specialists as co-navigators for families with substance use history. Recovery specialists handle SUD-specific coordination while navigators handle economic mobility.

Why it works: Adds specialized capacity without increasing caseloads or diluting navigator focus.

Large Employer Partnership

Adaptation: Placed dedicated navigator on-site at regional hospital system (3,000+ employees). All new hires in entry-level positions offered navigation as part of onboarding.

Why it works: Embeds navigation into employment pipeline without changing core model.

Social Capital & Peer Support Integration

One critical adaptable element is how you build social capital and peer support into the model. While navigator relationships are non-negotiable, communities can add layers of peer connection that strengthen outcomes.

Why Social Capital Matters

Research from programs like Circles USA demonstrates that bridging social capital—connections to people outside one's immediate network who have access to different resources and opportunities—is a powerful accelerator of economic mobility. Families in poverty often have strong "bonding" social capital (close ties to family and neighbors) but limited "bridging" capital (connections to employers, professionals, and community leaders).

The Empower navigator relationship provides one form of bridging capital, but additional peer structures can multiply this effect.

Peer Support Options

Peer Cohort Model

Participants enroll in groups, meet regularly, and progress together through 18-24 month journey. Creates mutual accountability and shared learning.

High Impact

Alumni Mentorship

Graduates who have achieved self-sufficiency mentor current participants. Provides hope, practical wisdom, and proof that mobility is possible.

Moderate Effort

Cross-Class Partnerships (Circles)

A core component of the Empower UC model. Community volunteers ("Allies") from different economic backgrounds form intentional relationships with participants, expanding social networks beyond poverty and providing ongoing support throughout the journey.

Empower UC

Family Support Groups

Structured gatherings for participants and their families to share challenges, celebrate wins, and build community. Often includes shared meals.

Low Barrier

Employer Peer Networks

Within certified employers, connect program participants who work at the same company. Creates workplace support system and carpooling opportunities.

Workplace Based

Community Voice & Co-Design

Another adaptable element is how deeply you involve participants in program design and governance. While the Four Non-Negotiables must remain intact, the way you implement them should be shaped by community input:

Participant Advisory Board

Current participants and alumni serving on a formal advisory body that provides input on program operations, policies, and communications.

Participant Feedback Loops

Regular surveys, listening sessions, and feedback mechanisms that inform continuous improvement—not just exit surveys.

Co-Design Sessions

Including participants in the design of new services, materials, or approaches before implementation. Especially valuable when expanding to new populations.

Story-Sharing Platforms

Creating opportunities for participants to share their journeys (with consent) to humanize the program and inform policy advocacy.

Implementation Note

Social capital building takes time and intention. If you're launching a new program, start with the navigator model first and add peer structures as capacity allows. Don't dilute navigator relationships by overloading the model with too many components at once. A well-implemented core model beats a poorly-implemented comprehensive model.

Team Structures: The Empower Model

The Empower Model—part of the Poverty Alleviation System designed by The Poverty Solution—works from both directions: supporting families on the ground while simultaneously advocating for systemic and policy change at the community level. This requires intentional team structures that connect frontline work to leadership decision-making. Your community should implement these five components:

1

Economic Stability Teams

The frontline team working directly with families—navigators, case managers, and partner agency staff. This team coordinates services to remove barriers to livable wage employment, tracks family progress through the data dashboard, and surfaces real-time insights about what families are experiencing. They are the "bottom-up" intelligence of the model, identifying barriers and gaps as they emerge.

2

Collective Impact Teams

The critical middle layer that bridges frontline work and leadership. Collective Impact Teams listen to problems surfaced by Economic Stability Teams, develop shorter-term community solutions, and escalate major systemic issues to Leadership Teams for policy change. They work closely with pilot staff and community partners to problem-solve in real time—acting as the connective tissue between ground-level needs and strategic decisions.

3

Leadership Teams

Cross-sector leaders who hold the vision for the pilot and focus on longer-term solutions. Leadership Teams address systemic and policy change issues escalated by Collective Impact Teams, adjust the direction of the pilot as needed, and advocate for structural changes that make economic mobility possible at scale. This is the "top-down" component that turns frontline insights into lasting community change.

4

Advisory Board

Community leaders who provide oversight and support for the pilot. The Advisory Board brings diverse perspectives, opens doors to resources and relationships, and ensures the pilot stays accountable to the community it serves. Members contribute guidance and advocacy wherever they can—leveraging their influence to help the model succeed.

5

Employer Coalition

Certified employers and potential employers who come together monthly or quarterly to create job opportunities for participants. The coalition shares real-time intelligence on hiring needs, retention challenges, and workforce scarcity—and works together to solve employment gaps in the community. This ensures the model stays connected to actual livable wage jobs and that employers are actively invested in participant success.

Why all five matter:

Without Economic Stability Teams, you lose touch with what families need. Without Collective Impact Teams, problems get stuck between frontline and leadership. Without Leadership Teams, systemic barriers never change. Without the Advisory Board, the pilot lacks community accountability. Without the Employer Coalition, job placement becomes disconnected from real workforce demand. The Empower Model works because information flows across all five—families inform the system, leaders change policy, and employers create the livable wage opportunities families need.
