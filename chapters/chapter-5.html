Adaptable Components

Where you flex. Where you hold the line.

The Core Principle

The Four Non-Negotiables are locked. Everything else bends to fit your community, your existing systems, and your resources. This chapter draws a hard line between what must stay fixed for outcomes to hold and where you have real freedom to make the model your own.

Why This Distinction Matters

Every community that launches the Empower model will face pressure to "customize" it. Funders want their priorities front and center. Partners push shortcuts. Staff propose workarounds. Some of those changes are healthy -- they shape the model to local reality. Others gut the very outcomes you showed up to deliver.

You need to know the difference. We have watched well-intentioned tweaks destroy program effectiveness in other regions. This chapter gives you a clear line: where you flex and where you don't.

What the Research Shows

Implementation science draws a hard line between core components (the active ingredients that produce outcomes) and adaptable components (delivery mechanisms that shift based on context). Programs that define and protect their core components scale faster and hold their effectiveness across different settings.

Warning: Research shows 52% of nonprofits modify their programs to chase available funding -- often unknowingly gutting core components. This "funding-driven drift" is one of the top killers of programs during scale-up.

The Non-Negotiables (Fixed)

Quick reminder from Chapter 4: these four elements must stay intact for the model to work. They bear repeating here because as you adapt the Empower Method to your community, these are the lines you cannot cross. Compromise any one and outcomes drop:

1. Benefits Cliff Navigation

Every family gets personalized benefits cliff modeling before making employment decisions. No self-service tools. No generalized advice. Personal, specific, accurate.

2. Navigator Model (1:25 ratio max)

Navigators carry caseloads small enough for real relationships. Higher ratios turn support transactional. Transactional support loses families.

3. Wraparound Coordination

Every service connects to one goal: getting families into livable-wage careers. Navigators coordinate across systems to clear what stands in the way.

4. Data Dashboard Accountability

Real-time outcome tracking, visible to all stakeholders. No hiding poor results behind quarterly reports.

2 Targeting & Eligibility

The ALICE threshold (225% FPL) is recommended but adaptable based on your funding sources and community needs:

Income-Based Targeting

  • ALICE threshold (up to 225% FPL)
  • TANF-eligible families
  • Specific income bands (150-300% FPL)

Population-Based Targeting

  • Parents with children under 18
  • Specific employer workforces
  • Geographic zones (specific counties)

System-Based Entry Points

  • TANF recipients nearing time limits
  • Workforce program completers
  • Re-entry population

Employer-Based Targeting

  • Employees of certified employers
  • Industry-specific (healthcare, manufacturing)
  • New hires in first 90 days

3 Service Delivery Channels

How families reach navigators can look different in every community:

  • In-person at central location: Dedicated Empower office -- the traditional model
  • Mobile/itinerant navigators: Navigators go to families -- better for very rural, dispersed populations
  • Employer site-based: Navigators hold hours at certified employer locations
  • Hybrid digital: Initial screening online, intensive support face-to-face
  • Community anchor-based: Co-located with churches, libraries, or community centers

Decision Framework: When to Adapt

Stakeholders will propose changes. Use this framework to decide:

✓

Adapt if the change...

  • Fits local systems better without compromising core functions
  • Removes a real barrier families face in your community
  • Uses existing resources more efficiently
  • Makes the model more sustainable long-term
✗

Hold the line if the change...

  • Pushes caseload ratios beyond 1:25
  • Strips benefits cliff analysis from the process
  • Turns navigators into transactional processors instead of relational partners
  • Reduces data transparency or accountability
  • Puts funder preferences ahead of family outcomes
"The art of implementation is knowing where to be rigid and where to be flexible. Get it backwards and you either break the model or fail to fit the community."

Adaptation Examples

Real communities have adapted the model while keeping the core intact. Here is what that looks like:

Rural Appalachian Region

Adaptation: Added a mobile navigator van for communities with no public transportation. Navigators go to families instead of expecting families to come to a central office.

Why it works: Solves a real access barrier without changing the navigator relationship model.

Recovery-Focused Implementation

Adaptation: Paired peer recovery specialists with navigators for families with substance use history. Recovery specialists handle SUD-specific coordination. Navigators stay focused on economic mobility.

Why it works: Adds specialized capacity without increasing caseloads or diluting navigator focus.

Large Employer Partnership

Adaptation: Placed a dedicated navigator on-site at a regional hospital system (3,000+ employees). Every new hire in an entry-level position gets navigation as part of onboarding.

Why it works: Embeds navigation into the employment pipeline without touching the core model.

Social Capital & Peer Support Integration

One powerful adaptable element: how you build social capital and peer support into the model. Navigator relationships are non-negotiable. But communities can layer in peer connections that accelerate outcomes.

Why Social Capital Matters

Research from programs like Circles USA shows that bridging social capital -- connections to people outside your immediate network who hold different resources and opportunities -- is a powerful accelerator of economic mobility. Families in poverty often have strong "bonding" social capital (close ties to family and neighbors) but limited "bridging" capital (connections to employers, professionals, and community leaders).

The Empower navigator relationship provides one form of bridging capital. Additional peer structures multiply that effect.

Peer Support Options

Peer Cohort Model

Families enroll in groups, meet regularly, and move through the 18-24 month journey together. Creates mutual accountability and shared learning.

High Impact

Alumni Mentorship

Graduates who reached self-sufficiency mentor current families. Proof that mobility is real. Practical wisdom from someone who has been there.

Moderate Effort

Cross-Class Partnerships (Circles)

A core component of the Empower UC model. Community volunteers ("Allies") from different economic backgrounds form intentional relationships with families, expanding social networks beyond poverty and providing ongoing support throughout the journey.

Empower UC

Family Support Groups

Structured gatherings where families share challenges, celebrate wins, and build community. Often includes shared meals.

Low Barrier

Employer Peer Networks

Connect families in the program who work at the same certified employer. Creates workplace support and carpooling opportunities.

Workplace Based

Community Voice & Co-Design

Another adaptable element: how deeply you involve families in program design and governance. The Four Non-Negotiables stay fixed. How you implement them should be shaped by the people they serve:

Family Advisory Board

Current families and alumni on a formal advisory body that shapes program operations, policies, and communications.

Family Feedback Loops

Regular surveys, listening sessions, and feedback channels that drive continuous improvement -- not just exit surveys after the fact.

Co-Design Sessions

Bring families into the design of new services, materials, or approaches before you launch them. Especially valuable when expanding to new populations.

Story-Sharing Platforms

Creating space for families to share their journeys (with consent) to humanize the program and fuel policy advocacy.

Implementation Note

Social capital takes time and intention to build. If you are launching a new program, start with the navigator model first. Add peer structures as capacity grows. Do not dilute navigator relationships by overloading the model with too many moving parts at once. A well-executed core model beats a half-built comprehensive one every time.

Team Structures: The Empower Poverty Alleviation System

The Empower Poverty Alleviation System -- built on the framework designed by The Poverty Solution -- works from both directions: supporting families on the ground while pushing for systemic and policy change at the community level. That requires intentional team structures connecting frontline work to leadership decisions. Your community needs these five components:

1

Economic Stability Teams

The frontline team working directly with families -- navigators, support staff, and partner agency personnel. This team identifies and clears specific barriers to livable-wage careers (benefits cliffs, childcare gaps, transportation, credentials, health issues), tracks family progress through the data dashboard, and escalates systemic barriers to Collective Impact Teams. They are the "bottom-up" intelligence of the model -- translating what families experience into actionable insights for the entire system.

2

Collective Impact Teams

The critical middle layer bridging frontline work and leadership. Collective Impact Teams receive problem reports from Economic Stability Teams, triage issues, and determine what gets solved through immediate operational adjustments -- convening partner agencies, reallocating resources, or running problem-solving sessions. When barriers hit multiple families or require policy change, they escalate to Leadership Teams. They act as both problem-solvers (for tactical, community-level barriers) and translators (connecting ground-level reality to strategic decision-makers).

3

Leadership Teams

Cross-sector leaders who hold the vision and steer the pilot as circumstances shift. Leadership Teams tackle longer-term solutions and systemic change -- policy barriers (childcare regulations, transportation infrastructure, benefits cliff design), structural reforms, and keeping the pilot connected to its core mission. They turn frontline insights into lasting community change. They champion the systemic shifts that make economic mobility possible at scale.

4

Advisory Board

Community leaders who oversee the pilot and hold it accountable. The Advisory Board monitors progress against outcomes, represents community interests, and opens doors to resources and relationships. Unlike the Employer Coalition's focus on livable-wage careers, the Advisory Board keeps the pilot true to its mission -- creating genuine economic mobility, not just activity. Members use their influence to advocate for the families the pilot serves.

5

Employer Coalition

Certified employers and prospective employers -- focused exclusively on employment infrastructure -- who meet monthly or quarterly to create livable-wage career opportunities for families. The coalition shares real-time intelligence on hiring needs, retention challenges, and workforce scarcity, and works together to close employment gaps in the community. This keeps the model connected to actual livable-wage careers and ensures employers are actively invested in family success.

Why all five matter:

Without Economic Stability Teams, you lose touch with what families need. Without Collective Impact Teams, problems get stuck between frontline and leadership. Without Leadership Teams, systemic barriers never change. Without the Advisory Board, the pilot has no community accountability. Without the Employer Coalition, job placement disconnects from real workforce demand. The Empower Model works because information flows across all five -- families inform the system, leaders change policy, and employers create the livable-wage opportunities families need.
