Why Scale Matters Now

The window for rural economic mobility infrastructure is open—but not indefinitely. Three forces are converging:

1

Workforce Crisis Hitting Critical Mass

Rural employers across every sector report inability to fill positions. Manufacturing, healthcare, agriculture—all facing the same cliff. The political will for solutions has never been higher.

2

Benefits Cliff Awareness Rising

What was once an insider policy conversation is now mainstream. State governments are actively seeking solutions. The Atlanta Fed's benefits cliff tools have created shared language.

3

Funder Fatigue with Failed Urban Exports

National foundations have spent decades funding "innovative" programs that fail when transplanted to rural contexts. There's growing appetite for rural-native solutions with evidence.

The Scaling Model

Most nonprofit scaling follows a flawed playbook: prove it locally, then try to convince others to adopt it. This rarely works because:

  • Local success factors are often invisible and undocumented
  • Replicators lack the support to implement correctly
  • No one owns quality control across sites
  • Each site reinvents infrastructure from scratch

The Empower scaling model is different. We're not asking communities to adopt and adapt on their own. We're building tiered infrastructure that supports quality implementation at every level.

Three-Tier Scaling Architecture

Tier 1: National Hub

The Poverty Solution

  • • Maintains the Empower methodology and quality standards
  • • Develops and licenses technology platform (benefits modeling, dashboards)
  • • Trains and certifies regional implementation partners
  • • Conducts cross-site research and continuous improvement
  • • Manages national funder relationships and advocacy

Tier 2: Regional Anchors

Development Districts, Community Action Agencies, Regional Intermediaries

  • • Implements Empower model across multi-county service area
  • • Hires, trains, and supervises navigators
  • • Manages employer certification program locally
  • • Coordinates with existing workforce and social service systems
  • • Reports outcomes to national hub and local funders

Tier 3: Community Partners

Employers, Human Service Agencies, Healthcare Providers

  • • Participates in Certified Employer or Referring Partner network
  • • Receives training through Poverty IQ program
  • • Integrates navigator touchpoints into existing operations
  • • Contributes to shared data and outcome tracking
  • • Benefits from network effects of regional coordination

What Makes Scaling Succeed

We've studied why promising programs fail to scale. The Empower model is designed around avoiding these failure modes:

Failure Mode #1: Founder Dependence

The problem: Success relies on the charisma and relationships of one leader. When they leave, the model collapses.

Our solution: The Four Non-Negotiables aren't about people—they're about systems. Any region can implement benefits cliff navigation, the navigator model, wraparound coordination, and data dashboards with the right training and support.

Failure Mode #2: Context Blindness

The problem: Models developed in one context assume their specific conditions are universal. Urban programs assume public transit. Coastal programs assume diverse industries.

Our solution: Rural-native design from day one. Every element of the model was built for low-density, relationship-based, limited-infrastructure contexts. We're not adapting urban models—we're creating the rural standard.

Failure Mode #3: Replication Without Support

The problem: "Here's a toolkit, good luck" doesn't work. Local implementers face unexpected challenges and have no one to call.

Our solution: The three-tier architecture ensures every regional anchor has direct support from the national hub. Monthly coaching calls, annual in-person convenings, shared troubleshooting forums, and rapid-response technical assistance.

Failure Mode #4: Mission Drift Under Pressure

The problem: Funders or local stakeholders push programs to abandon core principles for short-term gains or political convenience.

Our solution: Clear certification requirements. Regional anchors that deviate from the Four Non-Negotiables lose their certification and network access. The brand is protected by quality standards, not just licensing agreements.

Regional Coalition Strategy

One of the most powerful—and often overlooked—paths to scaling is not going bigger, but going wider. Instead of expanding Empower as a single national organization, multiple regional pilots can form intentional coalitions that unlock funding opportunities impossible for individual programs to access.

Why Regional Coalitions Open New Funding Doors

Individual programs compete for the same limited local funding. A single navigator program in Upper Cumberland reaches hundreds of families. But when Upper Cumberland coordinates with neighboring development districts as a regional economic mobility ecosystem, something fundamentally changes:

  • State-level funders recognize system-level thinking. A coalition showing coordinated impact across multiple counties signals serious infrastructure investment, not a one-off pilot.
  • Federal doors open. EDA, ARC, and workforce boards have much larger funding pots for regional initiatives than local programs can access.
  • Major foundations shift their calculus. Lumina, Gates, and other national foundations are actively seeking evidence-based models that can work at scale. A multi-county coalition with integrated data demonstrates scalability.
  • Employer recruitment becomes dramatically easier. A multi-county employer certification network is more attractive to national employers and supply chain partners than isolated local programs.

Example: Imagine Empower Upper Cumberland joining with Coal Region Development District (Pennsylvania), Eastern Kentucky Concentrated Employment Program (Kentucky), and North Georgia Development Authority (Georgia) as a formal "Appalachian Economic Mobility Coalition." Suddenly, you're not competing for $250K local grants—you're eligible for multi-million-dollar ARC funding, regional workforce development grants, and the attention of national foundations looking for rural-native infrastructure.

Coalition Structure Options

Lead Agency Model

One established organization (often largest development district) holds the grant and subcontracts to partners.

Best for: Quick coalition formation; clear accountability. Risk: Smaller partners feel secondary.

Fiscal Sponsor Model

A neutral backbone organization (community foundation, intermediary) holds funding and manages distribution equally.

Best for: Larger coalitions where partner equity matters. Risk: Slower decision-making; requires new entity.

Consortium Model

Shared governance through formal coalition council with rotating leadership. Each partner maintains independence while pooling resources.

Best for: Mature coalition with established trust. Risk: Complex governance; consensus-seeking can slow execution.

What Funders Want to See in Regional Coalitions

Not all coalitions are created equal. Funders—especially those managing seven-figure grants—have learned to distinguish between genuine regional infrastructure and administrative convenience coalitions. Here's what they're evaluating:

  • Shared Outcomes Framework. Not "we each do our own thing and report separately." Instead: integrated target population definition, aligned performance metrics across all partners, and collective accountability for regional economic mobility gains.
  • Clear Roles and Responsibilities. Which partner handles employer certification? Who manages the shared benefits counselor network? Where does the data live? Written MOU, not handshake agreements.
  • Data Sharing Agreements. A regional dashboard showing not just individual site performance but collective impact. HIPAA/FERPA compliant cross-site data integration that reveals patterns invisible at single sites.
  • Demonstrated Coordination, Not Co-Location. Having quarterly steering committee meetings where partners report to each other separately is theater. Actual coordination means: shared employer recruitment, navigators who refer across county lines based on job placement opportunity (not administrative convenience), and genuine resource pooling.
  • Sustainability Beyond Initial Grant Period. How will the coalition sustain after grant money ends? Are there earned revenue models? State funding commitments? National certification fee structures that support ongoing operations?

Funder Red Flags & Green Lights

🚩 Red Flags

  • • "We're a coalition but each partner operates independently"
  • • No shared data infrastructure
  • • Coalition formed primarily to access grant (dissolves when money ends)
  • • Partners don't know each other's metrics
  • • No written agreements on resource sharing

✓ Green Lights

  • • Shared KPI dashboard updated monthly
  • • Cross-county case conferencing
  • • Joint employer recruitment strategy
  • • Revenue model not 100% grant-dependent
  • • Coalition formed by stakeholder demand (not funder requirement)

Building Coalition Infrastructure

Regional coalitions don't just happen. They require intentional infrastructure investment, especially in the first two years:

  • Start with informal coordination before formalizing. Six months of monthly coordination calls between executive directors, shared learning about each other's challenges, and informal resource sharing. Only when trust exists should you formalize through MOUs or fiscal sponsors.
  • Create a shared assessment or data dashboard. Invest in integrated systems that show regional economic mobility outcomes—not just individual program metrics. This becomes your primary advocacy and funder communication tool.
  • Establish regular leadership alignment meetings. Monthly executive director calls focused on strategic issues (funder relations, employer partnership expansion, policy engagement). Quarterly in-person convenings where full teams (navigators, benefits counselors, employers) can coordinate directly.
  • Develop a combined identity and communication strategy. A shared website describing the regional coalition, a unified slide deck that can be customized for each partner's local context, and a joint landing page for employers interested in multi-state certification.
  • Invest in backbone staff infrastructure. For coalitions of 3-5 regional anchors, consider a $100-150K annual role: Coalition Coordinator (or "Director of Regional Alignment"). This person manages data integration, coordinates meetings, troubleshoots tensions, and keeps the coalition focused on shared outcomes.

Funding Sources for Regional Coalition Approaches

Once your coalition is structured, you become eligible for funding streams unavailable to single-site programs:

Economic Development Administration (EDA)

Multi-million-dollar grants for regional economic development projects. EDA explicitly prioritizes workforce development as component of economic strategy. Regional coalition structure is ideal for EDA's "regions" mandate.

Appalachian Regional Commission (ARC)

Up to $2M per approved project for multi-county regional initiatives in Appalachian region. Focuses on workforce development, economic opportunity, and cross-sector coordination.

State Workforce Development Boards

Each state distributes Workforce Innovation and Opportunity Act (WIOA) funds to regional boards. Regional coalitions can apply for local area grants ($1-5M+) or state-level innovation grants.

Major National Foundations

Lumina Foundation, Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation, Ford Foundation, and others with "rural" or "economic opportunity" priorities. Multi-site coalitions demonstrate scalability and systems thinking these funders want to support.

State TANF Flexibility Funds

Most states allocate Temporary Assistance to Needy Families dollars to innovative workforce/poverty reduction projects at state level. Regional coalitions focused on TANF populations can access significant flexible funding.

Coalition Funding Strategy Example: A 4-county coalition applying together could realistically pursue: $1.2M EDA regional planning grant (2-year) + $800K state workforce board grant (1-year, renewable) + $600K foundation grant (2-year) = $2.6M in first 24 months—far beyond what any single site could access independently.
