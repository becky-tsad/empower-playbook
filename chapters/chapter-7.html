The Phased Approach

Building infrastructure before serving families

Implementation takes time. The Empower Method uses a phased approach that builds infrastructure before serving families—ensuring that when participants arrive, the system is ready for them.

Why Phasing Matters: Evidence from Scale-Up Research

Programs that rush to scale typically see reduced effect sizes when moving from controlled trials to real-world implementation. The phased approach helps maintain quality during expansion.

NFP Success Story: Lower Costs at Scale

The Nurse-Family Partnership achieved something rare: lower per-participant costs at scale ($8,860) compared to original trials ($12,398). This counterintuitive result came from disciplined phased implementation—building infrastructure properly before expanding services.

Critical insight: You should NOT enroll participants in the first 6 months. This phase is about building the foundation. Rushing to serve families before your infrastructure is ready creates the exact fragmentation you're trying to solve.

Phase 1: Foundation (Months 0-6)

Goal: Build the infrastructure. No families enrolled yet.

This phase is about getting your house in order. You're creating the shared vision, hiring the right people, training them, establishing partnerships, and setting up systems. Skip this phase and you'll spend years cleaning up the mess.

Phase 1 Checklist

Success Markers for Phase 1

You're ready for Phase 2 when you can check these boxes:

  • Shared vision document signed by all partners
  • All staff hired and trained (certifications complete)
  • Dashboard live and tested
  • 3-5 employers in certification pipeline (at least 2 certified)
  • Partner agreements in place
  • Team can articulate the Four Non-Negotiables without hesitation

Phase 2: Activation (Months 6-12)

Goal: Start serving families and proving the model.

Now you're ready to bring participants into the system. Start small—this is still a learning phase. You're testing your processes, identifying friction, and refining before scaling.

Phase 2 Checklist

Success Markers for Phase 2

  • X families enrolled with complete intake data
  • X employers certified
  • First job placements documented
  • Regular coordination rhythm established and maintained
  • No families lost to fragmentation (everyone has an active navigator)
  • Early income gains visible in dashboard

Phase 3: Momentum (Months 12-24)

Goal: Scale what's working, fix what isn't.

By now you've learned what works in your community. Phase 3 is about scaling the model while maintaining quality. This is also when you start seeing graduates—families who have crossed the ALICE threshold and achieved self-sufficiency.

Phase 3 Checklist

Success Markers for Phase 3

  • Participants graduating above ALICE threshold
  • System running without daily firefighting
  • Documented ROI for funders
  • Waiting list for employer certification
  • Stories of transformation documented
  • Funding runway of 12+ months secured

The Minimum Viable Implementation

Not every community starts with full funding. If you're resource-constrained, here's how to phase the phases:

Starter

  • 1 Navigator
  • Benefits cliff tool
  • Basic tracking (spreadsheet OK)
  • Serve 25-50 families

Growth

  • 2-3 Navigators
  • Full dashboard
  • 5+ certified employers
  • Serve 100-200 families

Full Model

  • Full team (see Ch. 8)
  • All Four Non-Negotiables
  • 15+ certified employers
  • Serve 500+ families

Key principle: Start where you can, but don't compromise the Four Non-Negotiables. A small implementation with all four is better than a large implementation missing one.

Key Takeaway

The phased approach protects both families and your team:

  • Phase 1 (0-6 mo): Build infrastructure. No families yet.
  • Phase 2 (6-12 mo): Small cohort. Learn and refine.
  • Phase 3 (12-24 mo): Scale what works. First graduates.

Rushing to serve families before infrastructure is ready creates the fragmentation you're trying to solve. Take the time to build it right.
