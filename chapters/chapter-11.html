Adapting the Model

How the Four Non-Negotiables translate to any agency, program, workplace, or school system—with step-by-step implementation guides for each sector.

The Empower Method isn't locked to TANF populations in rural Tennessee. The Four Non-Negotiables are systems infrastructure—they describe HOW you coordinate support, not WHAT support you provide. This makes them adaptable to any organization working with people navigating economic instability, educational barriers, or life transitions.

11.1 Social Services Agency Adaptations

Community Action

Human Resource Agencies (HRA)

Download HRA Guide

Community Action Agencies and HRAs already serve ALICE populations across multiple program areas. The Empower Method provides the coordination layer they're missing.

How the Four Non-Negotiables Apply:

1

Benefits Cliff Navigation

Use existing LIHEAP, weatherization, and housing data to map complete benefits picture. Add cliff modeling before employment referrals.

2

Navigator Model

Convert existing case managers to navigators. Key shift: same person stays with family across ALL programs.

3

Wraparound Coordination

HRAs already touch housing, energy, food. Empower aligns all services toward one goal: livable wage employment.

4

Data Dashboard

Unify existing program databases. Create single family view. Track outcomes, not just enrollments.

Key Insight: HRAs often have the programs but lack the coordination. Empower provides the "connective tissue" to make existing services work as a system.

Recovery Services

Substance Abuse Recovery Programs

Download Recovery Guide

"Empower and recovery models are essentially the same work, just at different stages of a person's journey." Recovery stabilizes first; Empower provides the economic mobility pathway that follows.

Research: Recovery Employment Evidence

IPS Model

Most evidence-based employment model for recovery populations

13.4% to 2.9%

Uninsured rate drop for opioid hospitalizations in Medicaid expansion states

Critical Integration Point

Many in recovery receive Medicaid, SNAP, disability benefits. Cliff navigation prevents employment from triggering benefit loss during the vulnerable transition period—when losing healthcare or housing support could trigger relapse. Individual Placement and Support (IPS) is the most evidence-based employment model for recovery populations.

Key Insight: Recovery programs focus on stabilization; Empower provides the "what comes next" pathway. In Medicaid expansion states, uninsured rates for opioid-related hospitalizations dropped from 13.4% to 2.9%—demonstrating the critical role of healthcare access in recovery success.

Justice-Involved

Re-Entry Programs (Returning Citizens)

Download Re-Entry Guide

Returning citizens face all standard barriers plus additional ones: criminal record, supervision requirements, gaps in work history, employer hesitancy. The Empower Method provides structure for this complex navigation.

Why Navigator Model is Critical: Returning citizens have been failed by institutions repeatedly. Trust-based navigation over 18-24 months is essential. This population needs consistency, not handoffs.

Key Insight: Employer certification is especially critical here. Certified employers understand background check navigation and commit to fair-chance hiring practices.

11.2 Education System Adaptations

Here's the breakthrough: the same barriers that trap adults in poverty are the same barriers that prevent children from graduating. Family economic instability directly impacts student attendance, performance, and graduation rates. The Empower Method can transform educational outcomes by wrapping support around the entire family—not just the student.

K-12 Education

Student Success & Graduation Navigator

Download K-12 Guide

The Hidden Connection

Research shows that family economic instability is the #1 predictor of dropout risk—more than academic performance. When parents face eviction, lose childcare for siblings, or work multiple jobs, students miss school, can't focus, and eventually disengage. Addressing family barriers IS addressing student success.

How the Four Non-Negotiables Apply to K-12:

1

Benefits Cliff Navigation → Family Stability Planning

When families face benefits cliffs, students face attendance cliffs. Help parents navigate income changes so children don't become collateral damage. Partner with workforce agencies to support parent employment.

2

Navigator Model → Family Success Coach

One Family Success Coach per 30-40 at-risk families. Same person for 4+ years (elementary through middle school transition, middle through high school). Builds trust that traditional guidance counselors can't.

3

Wraparound Coordination → Community Schools Model

Coordinate all services toward family economic stability: housing, food, healthcare, childcare, transportation—all supporting parents' path to livable wage jobs.

4

Data Dashboard → Early Warning System

Track: attendance patterns, grade trends, behavior incidents, family stability indicators. Predict dropout risk 2-3 years before it happens. Intervene early.

Students whose families receive navigator support are 2.5x more likely to enroll in post-secondary education. By addressing family economic barriers, you're not just improving graduation rates—you're building the pipeline to college and career success.

Ages 0-5

Early Childhood & Head Start

Download Early Childhood Guide

Head Start already embraces two-generation approaches. The Empower Method provides the economic mobility infrastructure that makes parent services transformational rather than supplemental.

The Childcare Cliff Crisis

Childcare subsidies have some of the steepest benefits cliffs. A $0.50/hour raise can cost a family $8,000+ in childcare benefits. Early childhood programs are perfectly positioned to help families navigate these cliffs BEFORE they lose childcare.

Key Insight: When you stabilize the family during ages 0-5, you set the trajectory for K-12 success. Early intervention has the highest ROI of any education investment.

11.4 Workplace Adaptation: Supervisor-as-Navigator

Here's the breakthrough insight: the Empower Method doesn't require a social service agency to implement. Any employer with ALICE workers can adopt these principles—with supervisors serving as workplace navigators.

The Business Case

The average cost of turnover for an hourly employee is $3,500-$5,000. If benefits cliffs cause 20% of your ALICE workforce to leave (or refuse promotions), that's massive hidden cost. Supervisor-navigator training pays for itself in reduced turnover.

11.5 International Adaptation: Canada and Beyond

The Empower Method's power lies in its universal design. Benefits cliffs exist everywhere there are social safety nets. The Four Non-Negotiables translate across borders—what changes is the language, agency names, and specific benefit structures, not the core approach.

International

Canada: Provincial Benefits, Federal Opportunity

Download Canada Adaptation Guide

Why Canada First?

Circles Canada already operates in Toronto and other communities—this is proven, fertile ground. The infrastructure exists. What's missing is the economic navigation layer that Empower provides.

What's Similar to US
  • • Benefits cliffs (different names)
  • • Fragmented benefit programs
  • • Provincial/federal split
  • • Workforce boards exist
  • • Human services agencies
Canada-Specific Translation
  • • Ontario Works = TANF equivalent
  • • Disability Support Program = state disability
  • • Provincial social services ministries
  • • Employment Ontario = workforce system
  • • More healthcare coverage baseline
Strategic Advantage
  • • Federal poverty alleviation focus
  • • Liberal governments prioritize funding
  • • Smaller geography = faster scaling
  • • Fewer competing models
  • • First-mover advantage
Critical Research Question

The US has the Atlanta Federal Reserve CLIFF Dashboard—a comprehensive, publicly available tool showing exactly where benefits cliffs occur in every state.

Question for Canadian adaptation: Does Canada have an equivalent research tool that maps provincial benefit cliffs? If not, this is an opportunity: partnership with Canadian research institutions to build it first.

The Market Insight

"If we can get our foot in the door with Canadian communities, once federal funding starts to flow for poverty alleviation, we could capture significant market share in a way that would be impossible in the fragmented US system. We're essentially building a Canadian playbook with a proven US model."

Adaptation Playbook for Any Country

1
Research Local Benefit Structures

Map all social safety net programs, eligibility thresholds, phase-out rates. Identify cliff points. Understand the terminology—benefits have different names everywhere.

2
Identify Equivalent Agencies & Partners

Find the local versions of workforce development, social services, housing, healthcare. They exist—they're just named differently. Build relationships with these anchors.

3
Translate Language, Maintain Principles

Swap terminology (Ontario Works instead of TANF) but keep the core Four Non-Negotiables intact. The navigation approach is universal; the program names change.

4
Start with Existing Models

Look for local organizations already doing wraparound work (like Circles Canada). Partner and layer Empower's navigation precision on top of existing infrastructure.

The proof point is simple: if the model works with TANF populations in rural Tennessee, it works anywhere people face benefits cliffs and fragmented services. The Four Non-Negotiables—benefits cliff navigation, navigator relationships, wraparound coordination, and data-driven decisions—are universal truths.

11.6 What Stays the Same vs. What Adapts

Non-Negotiable (Never Changes)

  • ✓ Benefits cliff navigation — Always model before income changes
  • ✓ Sustained relationship — One person stays through the journey
  • ✓ Whole-person view — Address interconnected barriers
  • ✓ Data-driven decisions — Track outcomes, not activities
  • ✓ Self-sufficiency target — 225% FPL / ALICE threshold
  • ✓ Outcomes over outputs — Measure impact, not referrals

Adaptable (Context-Specific)

  • • Who serves as navigator — Case manager, supervisor, teacher, CHW
  • • Timeline — 18-24 months standard; longer for education/recovery
  • • Specific benefits tracked — Varies by population and state
  • • Wraparound partners — Different agencies per context
  • • Primary outcomes — Graduation, employment, housing, recovery
  • • Soft skills curriculum — Tailored to sector and population

Key Takeaway

The Empower Method is designed for adaptation. The Four Non-Negotiables are the DNA—they don't change. Everything else flexes to context:

✓ HRAs — Coordination infrastructure
✓ Schools — Graduation & college pipelines
✓ Recovery — Economic mobility after stabilization
✓ Healthcare — Address root causes of disparities
✓ Re-entry — Complex, multi-barrier navigation
✓ Employers — Supervisor-navigator retention
✓ Canada & Beyond — Universal benefit cliff principles

The core stays the same. The application adapts. That's how one model becomes a movement.

11.7 Tennessee Implementation

Tennessee presents a unique implementation environment. As a state without Medicaid expansion, with specific TANF time limits, and with a strong network of Development Districts, Tennessee-based programs must navigate distinct policy terrain. This section provides Tennessee-specific guidance for organizations implementing the Empower Method.

TennCare & Healthcare Navigation

Tennessee operates TennCare, not traditional Medicaid. Key distinctions for navigators:

Coverage Gaps

Adults ages 19-64 without dependents generally don't qualify for TennCare unless disabled. This creates a significant uninsured population earning too much for TennCare but too little for marketplace subsidies. Navigators must identify alternative healthcare access: community health centers, sliding-scale clinics, and employer coverage pathways.

TennCare Connect

Eligibility determination happens through TennCare Connect (tenncare.tn.gov). Processing typically takes 30-45 days. Navigators should submit applications early and have interim healthcare plans for participants.

CoverKids

Children's coverage extends to 250% FPL through CoverKids. This is ABOVE the ALICE threshold, meaning children can maintain coverage while parents increase income. Critical for cliff navigation planning.

Families First (TANF) Time Limits

Tennessee's TANF program (Families First) has strict time limits that affect planning horizons:

60 months

Lifetime federal limit

18 months

Continuous receipt limit (requires 3-month break)

Navigator Implication

Many participants have already used significant portions of their lifetime limit. Navigators must assess remaining TANF months early in engagement and build plans that don't assume continued TANF availability. The 18-month continuous limit means participants cycling on and off TANF—careful tracking prevents unexpected benefit termination.

Work Participation Requirements

Tennessee requires 35 hours/week of work activities for single parents (55 hours for two-parent households). Education alone doesn't count—must be combined with work. Navigators help participants structure schedules that meet requirements while pursuing credentials.

Tennessee-Specific Benefits Cliffs

Child Care Payment Assistance (CCPA)

Income eligibility up to 85% State Median Income. Tennessee uses a co-pay structure that increases with income—critical cliff to map. Waiting lists vary by county. Prioritize enrollment early in participant journey.

SNAP (TN)

Tennessee has NOT adopted broad-based categorical eligibility, meaning stricter income limits than some states. Gross income limit at 130% FPL. ABAWD work requirements apply in most counties.

LIHEAP & Utility Assistance

Low Income Home Energy Assistance through Human Resource Agencies (HRAs). Seasonal programs—fall applications for winter heating, spring for summer cooling. Build into annual planning cycles.
