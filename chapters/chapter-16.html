The Competitive Landscape

How Empower compares to other economic mobility models—and what makes our approach uniquely positioned for rural communities.

Economic mobility programs have evolved dramatically over the past two decades. From coaching models to peer support networks, from cash transfer programs to benefits cliff tools, the field is rich with innovation. Understanding where Empower fits—and what makes it different—is essential for funders, partners, and communities considering implementation.

16.1 Major Economic Mobility Models

The economic mobility field includes several well-established models, each with demonstrated outcomes and distinct approaches. Understanding their strengths helps clarify where Empower fills gaps in the current landscape.

Mobility Mentoring

EMPath (Economic Mobility Pathways)

Visit Website

EMPath pioneered the Mobility Mentoring approach, using brain science and economic frameworks to help families build economic stability across five domains.

Model Highlights
  • * 5-domain assessment (financial, education, employment, health, housing)
  • * Brain science-informed coaching
  • * Executive function skill-building
  • * Mobility Mentoring licensed nationally
Reported Outcomes
  • * 97% employed at program exit
  • * $32,548 average income at exit
  • * 25:1 caseload early, 60:1 later phases
  • * 18-24 month program duration
Gap Analysis
  • * No explicit benefits cliff focus as entry point
  • * Primarily urban-centric implementation
  • * Licensed model requires significant training investment
Coaching + Cash

LIFT

Visit Website

LIFT combines goal-based coaching with direct cash transfers to help parents build economic stability and break the cycle of intergenerational poverty.

Model Highlights
  • * Parent-focused (2-generation approach)
  • * $150/quarter direct cash transfers
  • * 2-year program duration
  • * Monthly contact with coaches
Reported Outcomes
  • * $21,325 average income increase
  • * 94% report improved financial situation
  • * 73% achieve primary goal
  • * Strong parent satisfaction scores
Gap Analysis
  • * Monthly contact (not intensive navigation)
  • * No specific benefits cliff expertise
  • * Urban locations only (Chicago, DC, LA, NYC)
Peer Volunteer Model

Circles USA

Visit Website

Circles builds intentional cross-class relationships between families working to increase income ("Circle Leaders") and middle-income volunteers ("Allies") who provide support and accountability.

Model Highlights
  • * Cross-class relationship building
  • * Weekly community meetings
  • * Peer support + volunteer "Allies"
  • * 18-month minimum commitment
Reported Outcomes
  • * 71% income increase at 18 months
  • * Strong social capital gains
  • * Community cohesion benefits
  • * 80+ communities nationwide
Gap Analysis
  • * Volunteer-based (not professional navigators)
  • * Less specialized benefits cliff knowledge
  • * Relies on consistent volunteer recruitment
Trust-Based Investment

Family Independence Initiative (UpTogether)

Visit Website

UpTogether (formerly FII) trusts families to drive their own solutions through unrestricted cash, peer support networks, and tracking tools—without prescribed interventions or case management.

Model Highlights
  • * Unrestricted cash transfers
  • * Self-determination philosophy
  • * Peer groups (no professional staff)
  • * Digital platform for tracking
Reported Outcomes
  • * $200M+ distributed to 200,000+ households
  • * Significant income and savings gains
  • * Strong evidence for cash transfers
  • * High family satisfaction
Gap Analysis
  • * Peer-only (no professional navigation)
  • * No benefits cliff expertise or modeling
  • * Cash alone doesn't address cliff barriers
Benefits Cliff Tool

Atlanta Fed CLIFF Tools

Visit Website

The Federal Reserve Bank of Atlanta developed CLIFF (Career Ladder Identifier and Financial Forecaster) tools to help workforce professionals and job seekers understand how income changes affect benefits.

Model Highlights
  • * Benefits cliff modeling calculators
  • * State-specific cliff data
  • * Free public tools
  • * Integration with workforce systems
Reported Outcomes
  • * 90% of users made action plans after coaching with tools
  • * Widely adopted by workforce boards
  • * Growing national awareness of cliffs
  • * Policy advocacy impact
Gap Analysis
  • * Tool-only (no integrated program model)
  • * No navigator relationship component
  • * Requires separate implementation infrastructure

16.3 The Five-Part Convergence

What makes Empower unique isn't any single element—it's the convergence of five elements that other programs treat as separate. Here's why each matters and why combining them creates something new.

1

Benefits Cliff as ENTRY POINT (Not Afterthought)

Most programs discover cliff barriers after placement fails. Empower starts with cliff analysis—mapping every benefit, modeling income scenarios, and planning transitions BEFORE making employment decisions.

Why it matters: A $2/hour raise that triggers $8,000 in childcare loss isn't a success—it's a trap. Starting with cliffs prevents "successful" placements that actually hurt families.

2

Professional Navigators (Not Peer Volunteers)

Peer models build social capital. Professional navigators build systems knowledge. ALICE families facing cliff barriers need someone who understands Medicaid redetermination timing, childcare subsidy income calculations, and employer benefit negotiation.

Why it matters: Navigating benefits requires technical expertise. A caring volunteer who doesn't understand 12-month lookback periods can give advice that costs families thousands.

3

Wraparound for Economic Mobility (Not Just Crisis or Child Welfare)

Traditional wraparound focuses on child welfare or behavioral health crises. Empower applies the same coordination intensity to economic mobility—treating poverty as the interconnected system it is.

Why it matters: You can't solve transportation in isolation when transportation connects to childcare connects to employment connects to benefits. Economic mobility requires the same systems coordination that crisis intervention receives.

4

Real-Time Data Dashboard (Not Retrospective)

Most programs report outcomes annually. Empower tracks metrics in real-time—so navigators see when a family is approaching a cliff, when engagement drops, when intervention is needed NOW.

Why it matters: Finding out a family fell off a cliff six months later doesn't help. Real-time visibility enables proactive intervention before crises become catastrophes.

5

Rural-Specific Design (Not Urban-Adapted)

Most economic mobility programs were built in cities and "scaled" to rural areas. Empower was built IN rural Tennessee, FOR rural communities—addressing transportation deserts, service scarcity, and the relationship-based trust that rural culture requires.

Why it matters: Urban solutions assume public transit, multiple service providers, and anonymity. Rural communities operate on personal relationships, geographic distances, and limited options. Different contexts require different designs.

Key Takeaway

The economic mobility field has strong models—but none designed specifically for rural communities facing benefits cliff barriers. Empower doesn't replace these models; it fills the gap they leave:

* EMPath provides coaching frameworks—Empower adds cliff expertise
* LIFT provides cash + coaching—Empower adds intensive navigation
* Circles builds community—Empower adds professional expertise
* UpTogether trusts families—Empower adds systems knowledge
* CLIFF Tools models cliffs—Empower adds full program infrastructure

Rural communities need a model built for them, not adapted to them. That's Empower.
