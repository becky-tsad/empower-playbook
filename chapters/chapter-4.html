Non-Negotiable #1: Benefits Cliff Navigation

What It Is

Benefits cliff navigation is proactive modeling of benefit transitions BEFORE families accept raises or new jobs. It's the difference between reacting to a crisis ("I just lost my childcare subsidy!") and preventing one ("If you take this raise, you'll lose $6,800--let's plan for that").

Why It's Non-Negotiable

The benefits cliff is the #1 reason working families stay trapped in poverty. Without explicit navigation:

  • Families unknowingly accept raises that make them poorer
  • Employers don't know how to promote people without hurting them
  • Well-meaning career advancement becomes a penalty

The Fear of the Cliff Is Just as Damaging

Many families refuse promotions or extra hours because they're afraid of hitting a cliff—even when the math would actually work in their favor. This fear-based paralysis keeps people stuck just as effectively as an actual cliff. Benefits cliff navigation isn't just about avoiding real cliffs; it's about helping families see when it's safe to climb and when to wait.

You cannot move families to self-sufficiency without addressing the cliff. Period.

How It Works

The Benefits Cliff Navigation Process

1

Map Current Benefits

Document every benefit the family receives: SNAP, Medicaid/TennCare, childcare subsidy, housing assistance, TANF, etc.

2

Identify Cliff Points

Calculate exactly where each benefit phases out or drops off entirely. Create a visual map of the danger zones.

3

Model Scenarios

When a job offer or raise comes, model the net impact: "This $3/hour raise will cost you $4,200 in childcare subsidy."

4

Create Transition Plan

Sequence income increases strategically. Maybe accept a smaller raise now, build savings, then make a bigger jump that clears multiple cliffs at once.

5

Coordinate with Employer

Work with certified employers to time raises, structure compensation, or provide benefits that offset cliff losses.

Minimum Requirement

Access to a benefits cliff calculator and trained staff who know how to use it. Every participant should have their personal cliff map before making any job or income decisions.

Non-Negotiable #2: The Navigator Model

What It Is

18-24 month relationships with dedicated coordinators who stay with families through their entire journey. Navigators are not case managers who refer; they are coordinators who stay.

Why It's Non-Negotiable

Trust takes time. Systems change takes longer.

When families have been failed by institutions before--and most have--they don't open up in the first meeting. They test you. They see if you'll disappear like everyone else. The relationship IS the intervention.

The Research on Trust-Based Relationships

Trust-based relationships produce an "amplifying effect" on outcomes. When participants trust their navigator:

  • They disclose real barriers (not just the ones they think won't be judged)
  • They follow through on difficult steps (because someone believes in them)
  • They weather setbacks without dropping out (the relationship provides stability)
  • They advocate for themselves with other systems (because they've seen it modeled)

Additionally, moving from crisis to stability to self-sufficiency isn't a 90-day process. It takes 18-24 months to:

  • Stabilize immediate crises (housing, childcare, transportation)
  • Address benefits cliffs strategically
  • Build skills and pursue better employment
  • Weather setbacks without falling back to square one
  • Reach true self-sufficiency (above ALICE threshold)

Critical: 18-24 Months Minimum

Research consistently shows that 18-24 months is the minimum duration for economic mobility impact. Shorter programs fail to produce lasting change—and relationships under 6 months can actually produce negative outcomes by creating dependency without building capacity.

Minimum Requirement

A dedicated navigator role (not split with case management duties). If your "navigators" are also doing traditional case management on a rotating basis, you don't have navigators--you have overloaded case managers with a new title.

Non-Negotiable #3: Wraparound Coordination

What It Is

Coordinated support that removes every barrier standing between a participant and a livable wage job. Housing, childcare, transportation, healthcare, financial coaching—all working together toward one goal: employment that pays enough to live on.

The Goal Is Clear: Livable Wage Employment

Every service we coordinate exists to answer one question: "What's keeping this person from getting and keeping a livable wage job?"

  • Housing instability? → Solve it so they can show up to work reliably
  • No childcare? → Solve it so they can accept full-time hours
  • Car trouble? → Solve it so they can get to the job site
  • Health crisis? → Solve it so they don't miss shifts or lose focus

The wraparound isn't the goal. The livable wage job is the goal. Wraparound removes the obstacles.

Why It's Non-Negotiable

You can't get someone into a livable wage job if their life is falling apart around them. And you can't address barriers one at a time—life doesn't wait:

  • Land a great job, but childcare falls through → lose the job
  • Get stable housing, but car breaks down → can't get to work → lose housing
  • Accept a promotion, but lose Medicaid → medical bill → spiral back to square one

Livable wage employment requires stability across multiple domains simultaneously. That's why we coordinate—not to check boxes, but to clear the path to a job that actually pays the bills.

Non-Negotiable #4: Data Dashboard

What It Is

Unified intake, tracking, and outcomes in one system accessible to all team members. Real-time visibility for navigators plus leadership reporting for strategic decisions.

Why It's Non-Negotiable

What gets measured gets managed. What gets shared gets coordinated.

Without unified data:

  • Navigators don't know what other agencies are doing with their families
  • Leadership can't see patterns or make strategic adjustments
  • Success stories get lost; failures repeat
  • Funders can't see ROI; funding disappears

What the Dashboard Includes

Intake Data
  • Family composition
  • Current income
  • Benefits received
  • Barriers identified
  • Goals set
Tracking Data
  • Navigator interactions
  • Services accessed
  • Progress toward goals
  • Barriers encountered
  • Benefits cliff status
Outcomes Data
  • Income changes
  • Employment status
  • Benefits transitions
  • ALICE threshold progress
  • Graduation status

Key Principle

Data for decisions, not just compliance.

The dashboard isn't for quarterly reports to funders (though it enables those). It's a daily tool for navigators and leadership to make better decisions in real time.

Minimum Requirement

A central tracking system accessible to all team members. This can start simple--even a well-structured spreadsheet--but it must be unified and shared. Siloed data defeats the purpose.
