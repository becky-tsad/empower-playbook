Building Your Team

The right people, in the right roles, doing the work that actually moves families out of poverty

Leadership: The Foundation of Everything

Before we talk about staffing or org charts, we need to talk about leadership. Without the right leader, nothing else in this chapter matters. Poverty is a "wicked problem"—complex, entangled, and designed to resist simple fixes. Solving it demands a different kind of leader.

In Beyond Poverty Management, Scott C. Miller identifies three leadership styles essential to this work:

1

The Visionary (Corporate Hero)

The visionary owns the mission and never stops sharing it. They keep the "why" front and center. They refuse to accept the status quo. This is leadership through clarity, confidence, and conviction—making sure every stakeholder knows what you're building and why it matters.

2

The Facilitator (Adaptive Leader)

The facilitator connects the big picture to the daily work. They bridge strategy and execution across teams and partners. It takes humility, curiosity, and emotional intelligence. Their job: keep everyone moving in the same direction.

3

The Practitioner (Collaborative Leader)

The practitioner turns vision into action. They build the checklists, create the systems, and make things happen. In the Empower model, this is the leader who translates the big picture into daily work—making sure families get served and outcomes get tracked.

Transformational Leadership = All Three

The best leaders in this work are fluent in all three styles. They know when to shift based on context, complexity, and urgency. As Miller writes: "Don't scatter your energy on a thousand good causes. Go all-in on the great one. Poverty is a wicked problem. It needs your whole heart, your full attention, and your most creative leadership."

Get leadership right, and the rest follows. Get it wrong, and even the best-designed program drifts. Here's the team you need to build.

Research-Based Staffing Standards

Successful navigation programs share clear staffing benchmarks:

Caseload Ratios

1:25 for intensive early-stage work with complex barriers; 1:15 or lower for families facing multiple compounding challenges (mental health, housing instability, substance recovery)

Training Requirements

Minimum 40 hours of initial training plus 2-3 months of supervised practice before navigators work independently. That's the threshold for competency in benefits cliff navigation, motivational interviewing, and trauma-informed care.

Ongoing Support

Weekly supervision + coaching is non-negotiable. Cut supervision frequency and outcomes degrade within 3-6 months. Every time.

Critical Warning

Staff turnover is the #1 killer of program quality. When experienced navigators leave and get replaced by undertrained staff, fidelity collapses. Invest in retention or watch your outcomes disappear.

Step 1: Hire a Poverty Alleviation Director

This is your first hire. Your most critical hire. Everything else flows from this role. The Poverty Alleviation Director leads the entire system—hiring teams, aligning partners, managing cross-sector relationships, overseeing quality, and holding fidelity to the model. This is not a program manager. This is a systems leader.

Poverty Alleviation Director

Core Responsibilities

Builds the plan: funding, resource allocation, timelines, risk management. Oversees the entire system—quality, reporting, and flow across all teams. Serves as the primary connector between the Leadership Team, Collective Impact Team, and Economic Stability Teams.

What to Look For

Someone who holds both the vision and the execution. They translate the Poverty Alleviation System into a practical, effective operation—weighing every decision for sustainability and scalability. This person leads across sectors and pushes through systemic barriers.

Estimated Salary

$95,000

Strongly Recommended: Assistant or Project Manager

Your Director needs support. Full stop. An assistant manages schedules and communication. A project manager handles day-to-day logistics, coordinates teams, and tracks timelines. Without this hire, your Director drowns in operational details instead of leading strategically.

Project Manager / Assistant

~$60,000

Step 2: Build Your Teams & Hire Navigators

Director hired. Now build the structure and hire frontline staff at the same time. The Poverty Alleviation System runs on three interconnected teams, plus the navigators (also called Prosperity Coaches) who work directly with families.

Leadership Team

Leaders from business, community, and government—including directors of state and federal agencies. These people have the influence and authority to change resource allocation, policy, and program administration. They also own the responsibility of securing sustainable funding beyond the pilot.

Includes: PAS Founder, Poverty Alleviation Director, department heads, policymakers, commissioners, key government influencers

Collective Impact Team

Drives collaboration between community leaders, experts, and stakeholders to design solutions, implement changes, and close service gaps. This team reviews data and stories from the Economic Stability Teams—learning what barriers families face in childcare, housing, healthcare, transportation, and employment—then reports to the Leadership Team to push for systemic change.

Includes: Community partners, leaders, experts, department heads, and members of the Leadership Team

Economic Stability Teams

The frontline. Dedicated navigators (Prosperity Coaches) and volunteers who walk with families through their Economic Stability Plans. This team empowers—never creates dependency. They help families build personalized plans and hold each other accountable. They document challenges, flag service gaps, and report up to the Collective Impact Team.

Includes: Navigators (Prosperity Coaches), Coach Leads, volunteers, and community partners

Navigators (Prosperity Coaches)

Navigators—called Prosperity Coaches in The Poverty Solution's training system—are the heart of the model. They work directly with families committed to getting out of poverty. That means knowing every regional service available, guiding families through their Economic Stability Plan, running Prosperity Meetings, providing ongoing coaching, and tracking every milestone. This is where the real work happens.

About These Salary Ranges

Here's the math that matters: replacing a navigator costs $54,000+. A $5,000 raise costs a fraction of that. Competitive pay isn't a luxury. It's how you keep the program running.

Tip: Hire from Existing Pilots When Possible

If your community ran a pilot, your best hires are already in the building. They know the philosophy. They have credibility with families and partners. They don't need retraining. Promote strong navigators to Coach Lead roles. Move pilot coordinators into leadership. These are your most proven people.

Where to Find Navigator Candidates

Rural hiring pools are small. You have to be creative and intentional about where you look:

Social Work & Human Services Programs

Partner with regional BSW/MSW programs and community college human services programs. Build internship-to-hire pipelines.

Community Health Workers

CHWs already know relationship-based support and community navigation. Certification program graduates make strong candidates.

Faith Communities

Churches often have members who care deeply about ending poverty. The mission alignment is already built in.

Recovery Community

People with lived recovery experience (2+ years stable) bring credibility you can't teach. They've walked the road.

Families Who've Made It Through

Program graduates who've achieved stability often become the best navigators. They know the journey because they lived it.

Retention: The Make-or-Break Factor

The Retention Reality

Staff turnover is the #1 cause of program drift. Nonprofit turnover runs at 19%—58% higher than other sectors—and 67% of staff are actively looking to leave. When experienced navigators walk out, fidelity collapses and family outcomes tank.

  • Competitive salaries — Replacing one navigator costs $54,000+. A $5,000 raise is cheaper than turnover. Every time.
  • Manageable caseloads — Never exceed 1:25. 70% of social workers report high emotional exhaustion. Overloaded navigators burn out and leave.
  • Weekly supervision — Cut supervision and outcomes degrade within 3-6 months. This is non-negotiable.
  • Ongoing development — 68% of employees would stay if they had training opportunities. Give them a reason to stay.

Proven Retention Strategies

Research points to specific interventions that cut turnover. These aren't nice-to-haves. They're the foundation of programs that last:

Build for Sustainability, Not Just Startup

The research is unambiguous: understaffing is the #1 reason collective impact initiatives fail. Don't staff for startup only. Plan for supervision ratios (1:6-8), retention strategies, and career pathways from day one. The programs that work—Nurse-Family Partnership, Communities in Schools, StriveTogether network members—all invest heavily in keeping their teams. Retaining great people is poverty alleviation.
