Building Your Team

The leadership and staffing that makes everything else work

Leadership: The Foundation of Everything

Before discussing staffing, roles, and team structure, we need to talk about leadership—because without the right leadership, none of the rest matters. Poverty is what researchers Horst Rittel and Melvin Webber called a "wicked problem": complex, entangled, and resistant to simple solutions. Solving it requires a different kind of leader.

In Beyond Poverty Management, Scott C. Miller identifies three leadership styles essential for this work, drawn from research on navigating wicked problems:

1

The Visionary (Corporate Hero)

The visionary holds the vision and continuously shares it with everyone involved. They keep the mission front and center, articulating urgency and refusing to accept the status quo. This is leadership through clarity, confidence, and conviction—ensuring that every stakeholder understands what you're building and why it matters.

2

The Facilitator (Adaptive Leader)

The facilitator sits in the middle, directing both the vision and the work being done. They connect the visionary's big picture to the practitioner's action items, ensuring alignment across teams and partners. It requires humility, curiosity, and emotional intelligence—bridging strategy with execution and keeping everyone moving in the same direction.

3

The Practitioner (Collaborative Leader)

The practitioner takes the vision and hits the ground running with action steps and to-dos. They translate strategy into execution—creating checklists, building systems, and making things happen. In the Empower model, this is the leader who turns the big picture into daily work, ensuring families are served and outcomes are tracked.

Transformational Leadership = All Three

The best leaders in this work are fluent in all three styles and know when to apply each depending on context, complexity, and urgency. As Miller writes: "Don't scatter your energy on a thousand good causes. Go all-in on the great one. Poverty is a wicked problem. It needs your whole heart, your full attention, and your most creative leadership."

With the right leadership in place, you can build a team that sustains the work. Without it, even the best-designed program will drift. Now let's look at the team you need to build.

Research-Based Staffing Standards

Evidence from successful navigation programs establishes clear benchmarks for effective staffing:

Caseload Ratios

1:25 for intensive early-stage work with complex barriers; 1:15 or lower for participants with multiple compounding challenges (mental health, housing instability, substance recovery)

Training Requirements

Minimum 40 hours of initial training plus 2-3 months of supervised practice before navigators work independently. This is the threshold for competency in benefits cliff navigation, motivational interviewing, and trauma-informed care.

Ongoing Support

Weekly supervision + coaching is essential for maintaining quality. Programs that reduce supervision frequency see measurable outcome degradation within 3-6 months.

Critical Warning

Staff turnover is the primary cause of program drift. When experienced navigators leave and are replaced by undertrained staff, fidelity erodes rapidly. Invest in retention.

Step 1: Hire a Poverty Alleviation Director

This is your first and most critical hire. Everything else flows from this role. The Poverty Alleviation Director leads the entire system—hiring teams, aligning partners, managing cross-sector relationships, overseeing quality, and ensuring fidelity to the model. This is not a program manager. This is a systems leader.

Poverty Alleviation Director

Core Responsibilities

Develops a comprehensive plan including funding, resource allocation, timelines, and risk management. Has oversight of the entire system including quality and flow of reports across all teams. Primary liaison between the Leadership Team, Collective Impact Team, and Economic Stability Teams.

What to Look For

Someone who can hold both the vision and the execution. They need to translate the Poverty Alleviation System into a practical, effective operation—evaluating sustainability and scalability of every decision. This person must be comfortable leading across sectors and navigating systemic barriers.

Estimated Salary

$95,000

Strongly Recommended: Assistant or Project Manager

It is critical that the Poverty Alleviation Director has adequate support. An assistant manages schedules and communication; a project manager oversees day-to-day logistics, coordinates teams, and tracks implementation timelines. Without this support, your Director will get pulled into operational details instead of leading strategically.

Project Manager / Assistant

~$60,000

Step 2: Build Your Teams & Hire Navigators

Once your Director is in place, the next phase is building the team structure and hiring frontline staff simultaneously. The Poverty Alleviation System runs on three interconnected teams, plus the Navigators (also called Prosperity Coaches) who work directly with families.

Leadership Team

Dedicated leaders from business, community, and government—including directors of state and federal agencies. These leaders possess the influence and authority to advocate for and implement changes in resource allocation, policy, and program administration. They are also responsible for securing sustainable funding beyond the pilot phase.

Includes: PAS Founder, Poverty Alleviation Director, department heads, policymakers, commissioners, key government influencers

Collective Impact Team

Facilitates collaboration between community leaders, experts, and stakeholders to design solutions, implement changes, and bridge service gaps. This team reviews data and stories from the Economic Stability Teams, gaining insights into the barriers families face in childcare, housing, healthcare, transportation, and employment—then reports to the Leadership Team to advocate for systemic change.

Includes: Community partners, leaders, experts, department heads, and members of the Leadership Team

Economic Stability Teams

The frontline of the system. Dedicated Navigators (Prosperity Coaches) and volunteers who guide individuals through their Economic Stability Plans. This team is trained to empower rather than create dependency—helping families develop personalized plans and serving as a source of accountability. They document challenges, identify service gaps, and report upward to the Collective Impact Team.

Includes: Navigators (Prosperity Coaches), Coach Leads, volunteers, and community partners

Navigators (Prosperity Coaches)

Navigators—called Prosperity Coaches in The Poverty Solution's training system—are the heart of the model. They provide direct financial navigation services to participants committed to getting out of poverty. This means understanding regional services, attending training on community resources, guiding families through their Economic Stability Plan, attending Prosperity Meetings, providing ongoing coaching, and completing onboarding and data entry.

About These Salary Ranges

These figures are validated against 2024-2026 BLS data and nonprofit compensation surveys. Rural organizations can expect to pay 15-25% below urban markets while remaining locally competitive. The investment in adequate compensation is cost-effective: replacing a Navigator costs $54,000+, while a $5,000 salary increase costs far less than turnover. Competitive pay isn't a luxury—it's the foundation of program sustainability.

Tip: Hire from Existing Pilots When Possible

If your community has run a pilot program, your best hires are often the people who already worked inside the model. They understand the philosophy, have credibility with families and partners, and don't need retraining. Promote strong Navigators to Coach Lead roles. Move pilot coordinators into leadership positions. These are your most proven candidates.

Where to Find Navigator Candidates

In rural areas, the hiring pool is limited. Be creative and intentional about where you look:

Social Work & Human Services Programs

Partner with regional university BSW/MSW programs and community college human services programs. Offer internship-to-hire pathways.

Community Health Workers

CHWs already understand relationship-based support and community navigation. Certification program graduates make excellent candidates.

Faith Communities

Churches often have members passionate about poverty alleviation. The mission-alignment is often already there.

Recovery Community

People with lived recovery experience (2+ years stable) bring irreplaceable credibility and understanding of the journey.

Former Participants

Program graduates who have achieved stability may become your best Navigators. They understand the journey intimately.

Retention: The Make-or-Break Factor

The Retention Reality

Staff turnover is the primary cause of program drift. Nonprofit turnover runs at 19%—58% higher than other sectors—and 67% of staff are actively looking to leave. When experienced Navigators depart, fidelity erodes rapidly and client outcomes suffer.

  • Competitive salaries — Replacing one Navigator costs $54,000+. A $5,000 raise costs far less than turnover.
  • Manageable caseloads — Never exceed 1:25. Research shows 70% of social workers report high emotional exhaustion.
  • Weekly supervision — Programs that reduce supervision see measurable outcome degradation within 3-6 months.
  • Ongoing development — 68% of employees would stay with additional training opportunities.

Build for Sustainability, Not Just Startup

The backbone organization research is clear: understaffing is the #1 reason collective impact initiatives fail. Don't build your team with just startup capacity in mind. Plan for supervision ratios (1:6-8), retention strategies, and career pathways from day one. The most successful poverty alleviation programs—Nurse-Family Partnership, Communities in Schools, StriveTogether network members—all invest heavily in team sustainability. Investment in retention is poverty alleviation.
