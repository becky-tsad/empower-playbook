What Kills Programs

(And How to Avoid It)

Most playbooks tell you what to do. This chapter tells you what NOT to do. These are the failure patterns we've seen kill promising programs—and the specific actions that prevent them.

Why this matters: Programs rarely fail dramatically. Research shows 91% lose effectiveness gradually—through drift, dysfunction, and accumulated small mistakes. The difference between programs that survive and those that fail is recognizing these patterns early and having the courage to intervene.

Research on Program Failure

Studies of evidence-based program implementation reveal consistent patterns in what causes programs to fail:

Funding-Driven Drift

52% of nonprofits modify their programs to fit available funding—often abandoning core components that produce outcomes in favor of activities funders prefer to fund.

Caseload Violations

Caseloads exceeding 1:30 correlate with poor outcomes across virtually all navigation program metrics. The navigator-participant relationship becomes transactional rather than transformational.

Relationship Duration

Navigator-participant relationships lasting under 6 months actually produce negative outcomes—worse than no intervention. Trust takes time to build.

Fidelity Monitoring Gaps

Programs without ongoing fidelity monitoring lose effectiveness within 12-18 months, then lose funding 6-12 months after that when outcomes data reveals the decline.

Common drift patterns: Quality reduction (cutting corners), fewer participant interactions, navigator reassignments to administrative tasks, and elimination of "expensive" components like supervision.

9.1 Coordination Collapse

!

The Symptom

Agencies working in silos. Families falling through gaps. Partner meetings become status updates where nothing changes. "That's not our department."

?

Why It Happens

Coordination is hard work that no one gets credit for. Each agency has its own reporting requirements, funding streams, and incentives. Without intentional effort, the default is silos.

+

The Prevention

  • Weekly coordination meetings (not monthly—weekly)
  • Shared data dashboard that everyone can see
  • Named point person for each family—one navigator owns the outcome
  • Agreements that include coordination commitments, not just service delivery

Case Study: When Coordination Fails Catastrophically

Victoria Climbié was seen by 3 housing authorities, 4 social services departments, 2 police child protection teams, an NSPCC specialist centre, and 2 hospitals. Despite all this contact, she died with 128 separate injuries on her body. The subsequent inquiry found that agencies failed to communicate, share information, or coordinate care. Each agency assumed someone else was handling it. This is what coordination collapse looks like at its worst—and why weekly coordination meetings, shared data, and named point persons aren't optional. Source: UK Victoria Climbié Inquiry

9.2 Communication Breakdown

!

The Symptom

Information dies in inboxes. No shared language across agencies. Updates happen but don't reach the people who need them. "I didn't know that changed."

?

Why It Happens

Everyone's busy. Communication takes effort. Without clear protocols, messages get lost. People assume someone else will share the update.

+

The Prevention

  • Shared terminology document—everyone uses the same words
  • Central communication channel (Slack, Teams, or similar)
  • Communication protocols: who needs to know what, when
  • Regular touchpoints that aren't optional

9.3 Duplicate Programs

!

The Symptom

Three agencies offer the same workshop. Four organizations provide food assistance. Everyone competes for the same grant dollars while critical gaps go unaddressed.

?

Why It Happens

Each organization builds programs based on available funding, not community need. Grant requirements often reward new programs over coordination. "We've always done it this way."

+

The Prevention

  • Complete service mapping in Phase 1—know what exists
  • Consolidate or specialize: "You do childcare, we do workforce"
  • Joint grant applications instead of competing
  • Courage to shut down programs that duplicate others

9.4 Funding as Afterthought

!

The Symptom

Constant survival mode. Grant deadlines are crises. Leadership spends more time fundraising than leading. "We'll figure out funding later."

?

Why It Happens

Program people want to do program work, not fundraising. Grant writing is skilled work that often falls to whoever has time. No one is dedicated to it.

+

The Prevention

  • Dedicated grant researcher/writer from Day 1
  • Pipeline math: pursue $4-5M to close $2.5M
  • 15-18 simultaneous foundation relationships
  • 12+ month funding runway at all times
  • Diversified funding (government + foundation + local)

The Nonprofit Starvation Cycle

Research from Stanford Social Innovation Review documents a vicious cycle: Funders cap overhead at 15%, but actual operating costs run 25-35%. Organizations cut infrastructure to maximize "program spending," which undermines organizational effectiveness, which weakens programs, which loses funders. 76% of nonprofits recover less than 10% in indirect costs. This math doesn't work—and it kills programs slowly. The solution isn't just "more funding"—it's diversified funding and explicit policies against chasing grants that compromise your model.

9.5 Vision Drift

!

The Symptom

Leadership starts aligned, then pulls in different directions. Each partner pursues their own interpretation. "That's not how I understood it."

?

Why It Happens

Verbal agreements fade. Pressure creates shortcuts. New people join and don't get the original vision. Time passes and priorities shift.

+

The Prevention

  • Documented shared vision—written, signed, referenced
  • Quarterly re-alignment sessions (not just status updates)
  • Decision framework that ties back to vision
  • Onboarding that includes vision immersion for new staff

9.6 Training-and-Forget

!

The Symptom

One onboarding session, then back to old habits. Staff can't remember the methodology. New hires learn from peers who learned from peers who learned from... someone who was trained.

?

Why It Happens

Training takes time away from "real work." Once people are "trained," that box is checked. The model evolves but training doesn't.

+

The Prevention

  • Ongoing training calendar (not just onboarding)
  • Return to shared vision as model evolves
  • Refresher sessions when methodology updates
  • Training is part of performance, not extra

The Research is Clear: Fidelity = Outcomes

A comprehensive review of 500+ studies found that programs with high implementation fidelity produce outcomes 2-3x higher than those with poor fidelity (Durlak & DuPre, 2008). Yet only 3.5% of interventions adequately monitor fidelity.

The 12-month window: Research shows the biggest fidelity gains happen in months 1-12. After that, without ongoing training and supervision, programs plateau and drift begins. The Nurse-Family Partnership assumes a 21.8% effectiveness decline whenever programs reduce service intensity. Training isn't a one-time event—it's infrastructure.

9.7 Checkbox Culture

!

The Symptom

Staff complete tasks but don't solve problems. Reports are filed but nothing changes. Compliance over outcomes. "I did what I was supposed to do."

?

Why It Happens

Systems reward completion over creativity. Risk-taking is punished. People optimize for what's measured—usually activities, not outcomes.

+

The Prevention

  • Hire for problem-solvers, not box-checkers
  • Interview question: "Tell me about a time you broke a rule to help someone"
  • Celebrate problem-solving in team meetings
  • Measure outcomes, not activities
  • Give navigators authority to make decisions

9.8 Understaffed Leadership

!

The Symptom

Everything bottlenecks at one or two people. Decisions wait weeks. Leaders burn out. Good ideas die in the queue.

?

Why It Happens

Programs staff for service delivery, not for leadership. "We'll add management capacity later when we grow." Leaders try to do it all.

+

The Prevention

  • Right-size leadership BEFORE launch
  • You need enough people who can actually MOVE things
  • Distribute decision-making authority
  • Staff for leadership, not just delivery
  • Build leadership depth—not a single point of failure

9.9 Lessons Learned from the Empower Pilot

The Empower pilot revealed critical insights about program implementation. These aren't theoretical—they're hard-won lessons from actual experience that directly shaped how we recommend building and sustaining programs.

Data Capture is Critical from Day One

The Lesson

If you have the wrong data being captured from the start, your pilot will be confusing and you won't be able to get more funding. This isn't a minor problem—it's a program killer.

What We Did Wrong Initially

  • Didn't define outcome metrics before launch
  • Control group design was muddled—support payments and coaching weren't cleanly separated
  • Built data infrastructure as an afterthought, not as foundational

What You Must Do

  • Define outcome metrics BEFORE launching—not after six months when you realize you're measuring the wrong things
  • Design control groups with intention—keep support payments equal while isolating navigator coaching, so you can actually measure the navigator's impact
  • Build data infrastructure first—this is foundational, not an afterthought. This is why we developed our own navigator data tool.
  • Work with a data expert from the design phase forward

Funding Cannot Be an Afterthought

The Hard Truth

Empower is facing staff layoffs because funding wasn't secured in advance. A program with proof of concept and positive outcomes is cutting staff because the leadership didn't plan for sustainability. This is tragic and preventable.

What Not to Do

  • Treat funding as "Phase 3" work when proof exists
  • Rely on a single funding stream
  • Wait until the grant is running out to start fundraising

What You Must Do

  • Build sustainability planning into Phase 1—this is when you design outcomes, it's when you design funding
  • Diversify funding sources from the beginning—government + foundation + local/private
  • Start grant relationship building simultaneously with program launch
  • Never let funding runway drop below 12 months

Leadership Alignment Takes Constant Work

The Reality Check

Vision drift happens when leadership isn't regularly aligned. You can have a brilliant shared vision statement, but if you don't continuously reinforce it, people drift back to their own interpretations and incentives.

Your Action Plan

  • Quarterly re-alignment sessions—not status updates, but real conversations about whether decisions align with vision
  • Shared vision requires ongoing reinforcement, not one-time agreement
  • Tie every major decision back to the vision statement
  • When new leadership joins, immersion in the vision is non-negotiable

Navigator Caseloads Creep Up

The Silent Killer

Without monitoring, caseloads slowly increase beyond effective ratios. It happens gradually: one more participant here, a partner referral there. Before anyone notices, your navigators are drowning and outcomes are declining.

How to Prevent It

  • Build caseload caps into policy, not just guidelines
  • Monitor actual caseloads monthly (not quarterly)
  • When caseloads hit 85% of maximum, pause new enrollment until you hire
  • Make caseload targets part of navigator performance metrics

Partner Coordination Requires Infrastructure

Infrastructure Isn't Boring

Informal partnerships break down under stress. You can have great relationships at launch, but when budgets tighten or leadership changes, those informal agreements evaporate. What worked during the honeymoon fails when it matters.

Build This Now

  • MOUs (Memoranda of Understanding) are non-negotiable—not bureaucratic busywork, but protection
  • Regular coordination meetings (weekly, not as-needed) to maintain relationships and address problems early
  • Shared data protocols so information flows reliably
  • Named point person at each partner organization

The Control Group Challenge

Theory vs. Reality

Original design: keep support payments equal, isolate navigator coaching as the variable you're testing. Sound reasonable? The reality was much harder to maintain than expected.

What Happened

The control group design eroded over time. Unpredictable contamination: some control group members got ad hoc navigator help, some treatment group members disengaged. Trying to maintain pristine separation was nearly impossible in a real community environment.

Our Workaround

  • Create categories based on actual navigator session frequency
  • Correlate session intensity with outcomes
  • Accept natural variation rather than fighting it
  • This gives you the data you need without unrealistic control group purity

Critical Lesson

Design evaluation methodology with a data expert from the start. This isn't something you figure out mid-pilot. Get research and evaluation expertise in the room when you're designing Phase 1.

Early Warning Signs

Research identifies predictive indicators that allow early intervention before failure becomes entrenched. If you see 3+ of these signs, intervene immediately:

Early Warning Checklist

□

Attendance/engagement declining at program activities or coordination meetings

□

Staff turnover accelerating (especially key positions in year 1)

□

Caseloads creeping above 1:25 without anyone raising the alarm

□

Supervision meetings becoming irregular or getting cancelled

□

Data collection becoming inconsistent or incomplete

□

Partners missing coordination meetings or sending substitutes

□

Funding conversations dominating strategy discussions

□

Staff saying "that's not how we do it anymore" about core model elements

Programs That Turned It Around

Not every struggling program fails. Here's what successful turnarounds look like:

Alcohol and Drug Council of Middle Tennessee

The crisis: Declining program quality, poor staff morale, organizational drift from previous leadership.

The turnaround:

  • New director interviewed every staff member individually
  • Adopted explicit "no mission-drift" funding policy—turned down grants that would compromise the model
  • Divested from programs that didn't fit core mission
  • Created visible quick wins (renovated meeting room in 2 months vs. year of committee debate)

Result: Organization survived, thrived, and became more mission-focused than ever.

Housing First Programs (National Scale)

The challenge: Funder requirements conflicted with core Housing First principles (requiring psychiatric assessment before housing).

The solution:

  • "Rule-bent" by shifting psychiatric assessment from first contact to after engagement—meeting compliance while protecting client choice
  • Daily team meetings reinforced Housing First philosophy
  • Mobile supervision and real-time team support

Result: Maintained 3.62/4 fidelity scores across diverse sites while meeting funder requirements. Source: PMC Housing First Fidelity Research

The Common Thread

Programs that turn around share three traits: (1) Leadership willing to make hard decisions, (2) Explicit policies protecting the model over chasing funding, and (3) Investment in fidelity monitoring and staff support. They don't drift—they course-correct.

The Bottom Line: Why Programs Really Fail

Programs fail not from lack of good intentions but from:

Structural Misalignment

Different rules, eligibility, and funding streams pulling partners apart

Perverse Incentives

Budgets and metrics that punish collaboration and reward silos

Fidelity Neglect

No one monitors whether the model is being followed (only 3.5% do)

Staff Instability

$54K per departure, 19% annual turnover—everything else breaks down

The programs that survive do three things differently: They monitor fidelity obsessively (weekly, not annually). They protect the model over chasing funding. They invest in staff retention as mission-critical infrastructure.

The Pattern Behind the Patterns

Notice what all these killers have in common: they're invisible until it's almost too late. Programs don't die from one catastrophic failure—they die from accumulated drift.

The prevention isn't heroic intervention. It's:

  • Regular check-ins against each pattern
  • Honest assessment of current state
  • Willingness to course-correct early
  • Leadership that watches for these signs

Use this chapter as a quarterly diagnostic. Are any of these patterns emerging? Address them now, not later.
