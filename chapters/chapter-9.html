What Kills Programs

(And How to Avoid It)

Most playbooks tell you what to build. This one tells you what will destroy it. These are the failure patterns that kill promising programs. We watched them happen. Here is how you stop them.

Why this matters

Programs don't explode. They erode. Research shows 91% lose effectiveness gradually--through drift, dysfunction, and a thousand small compromises nobody flags. The difference between programs that last and programs that die? Recognizing these patterns early. And having the guts to act.

What the Research Says About Program Death

Studies of evidence-based programs reveal the same killers over and over:

Funding-Driven Drift

52% of nonprofits reshape their programs to match whatever funders want. They abandon the components that actually produce outcomes. They chase money. Families pay the price.

Caseload Violations

Caseloads past 1:30 destroy outcomes across every metric. The navigator-family relationship becomes transactional. Navigators stop knowing families. Families stop trusting navigators. The whole model breaks.

Relationship Duration

Navigator-family relationships lasting under 6 months produce worse outcomes than no intervention at all. Read that again. Short relationships do more harm than doing nothing. Trust takes time.

Fidelity Monitoring Gaps

Stop monitoring fidelity and your program dies within 12-18 months. Then you lose funding 6-12 months after that, when the outcomes data finally reveals what everyone already felt.

How drift shows up: Corners get cut. Navigators see families less often. Navigators get pulled into admin work. "Expensive" components like supervision get eliminated first. Each cut seems small. Together, they gut the model.

9.1 Coordination Collapse

!

The Symptom

Agencies work in silos. Families fall through gaps. Partner meetings become status updates where nothing changes. "That's not our department."

?

Why It Happens

Coordination is hard. Nobody gets credit for it. Every agency has its own reporting, funding, and incentives. Without deliberate effort, the default is silos. Always.

+

The Prevention

  • Weekly coordination meetings. Not monthly. Weekly.
  • Shared data dashboard visible to everyone
  • One navigator owns the outcome for each family. Named. Accountable.
  • Partner agreements that require coordination, not just service delivery

When Coordination Fails, People Die

Victoria Climbie was seen by 3 housing authorities, 4 social services departments, 2 police child protection teams, an NSPCC specialist centre, and 2 hospitals. She died with 128 separate injuries on her body. Every agency assumed someone else was handling it. No one was. That is coordination collapse at its worst. That is why weekly meetings, shared data, and named point people are not optional. They are the difference between families getting help and families getting lost. Source: UK Victoria Climbie Inquiry

9.2 Communication Breakdown

!

The Symptom

Critical information dies in inboxes. Agencies speak different languages. Updates happen but never reach the people who need them. "I didn't know that changed."

?

Why It Happens

Everyone is busy. Communication takes effort nobody budgets for. Without clear protocols, messages vanish. People assume someone else shared the update. Nobody did.

+

The Prevention

  • Shared terminology document. Everyone uses the same words for the same things.
  • One central communication channel (Slack, Teams, whatever). Pick one. Use it.
  • Written protocols: who needs to know what, and when
  • Regular touchpoints that are mandatory, not optional

9.3 Duplicate Programs

!

The Symptom

Three agencies run the same workshop. Four organizations hand out food. Everyone fights over the same grant dollars. Meanwhile, critical gaps go unaddressed because nobody is filling them.

?

Why It Happens

Organizations build programs around available funding, not community need. Grants reward new programs over coordination. Turf protection is real. "We've always done it this way."

+

The Prevention

  • Map every service in Phase 1. Know what exists before you build anything.
  • Specialize: "You own childcare. We own workforce training."
  • Submit joint grant applications instead of competing against each other
  • Have the courage to shut down programs that duplicate what others already do well

9.4 Funding as Afterthought

!

The Symptom

Permanent survival mode. Every grant deadline is a crisis. Leaders spend more time fundraising than leading. "We'll figure out funding later." Later never comes.

?

Why It Happens

Program people want to do program work. Grant writing is specialized and time-consuming. Nobody is dedicated to it. So it falls to whoever has five minutes, which is nobody.

+

The Prevention

  • Hire a dedicated grant researcher/writer on Day 1
  • Pipeline math: pursue $4-5M to close $2.5M
  • Maintain 15-18 active foundation relationships at all times
  • Never let your funding runway drop below 12 months
  • Diversify: government + foundation + local. Never rely on one stream.

The Nonprofit Starvation Cycle

Stanford Social Innovation Review documented a death spiral: Funders cap overhead at 15%. Actual operating costs run 25-35%. Organizations gut infrastructure to inflate "program spending." Weak infrastructure undermines programs. Weak programs lose funders. 76% of nonprofits recover less than 10% in indirect costs. That math kills programs slowly. The fix is not just "more money." It is diversified funding and an explicit policy: never chase a grant that compromises your model.

9.5 Vision Drift

!

The Symptom

Leadership starts aligned. Then everyone drifts in different directions. Each partner runs their own version. "That's not how I understood it."

?

Why It Happens

Verbal agreements fade. Pressure creates shortcuts. New people join without context. Time passes. Priorities shift. The original vision becomes a memory, not a compass.

+

The Prevention

  • Write the vision down. Sign it. Reference it in every decision.
  • Quarterly re-alignment sessions (real conversations, not status updates)
  • A decision framework that ties back to the vision every time
  • New staff get vision immersion on day one. Non-negotiable.

9.6 Training-and-Forget

!

The Symptom

One onboarding session. Then old habits. Navigators forget the methodology. New hires learn from peers who learned from peers who vaguely remember someone who was actually trained.

?

Why It Happens

Training pulls people away from "real work." Once someone is "trained," the box is checked. The model evolves. The training stays frozen. Knowledge degrades like a game of telephone.

+

The Prevention

  • Build an ongoing training calendar. Onboarding is the start, not the end.
  • Return to shared vision every time the model evolves
  • Run refresher sessions when methodology updates
  • Training is part of performance expectations. It is not extra. It is the job.

The Research is Blunt: Fidelity = Outcomes

A review of 500+ studies found that programs with high implementation fidelity produce outcomes 2-3x higher than those with poor fidelity (Durlak & DuPre, 2008). Yet only 3.5% of programs adequately monitor fidelity. That number is staggering.

The 12-month window: The biggest fidelity gains happen in months 1-12. After that, without ongoing training and supervision, programs plateau and drift begins. The Nurse-Family Partnership assumes a 21.8% effectiveness decline whenever programs cut service intensity. Training is not a one-time event. It is infrastructure. Treat it that way or watch your outcomes decay.

9.7 Checkbox Culture

!

The Symptom

Navigators complete tasks but don't solve problems. Reports get filed. Nothing changes. Compliance replaces outcomes. "I did what I was supposed to do."

?

Why It Happens

Systems reward completion, not creativity. Risk-taking gets punished. People optimize for what gets measured. Usually that means activities, not whether families actually move forward.

+

The Prevention

  • Hire problem-solvers, not box-checkers
  • Ask in interviews: "Tell me about a time you broke a rule to help someone"
  • Celebrate problem-solving in team meetings. Publicly.
  • Measure outcomes for families, not activities completed
  • Give navigators real authority to make real decisions

9.8 Understaffed Leadership

!

The Symptom

Everything bottlenecks at one or two people. Decisions stall for weeks. Leaders burn out. Good ideas die waiting in line.

?

Why It Happens

Programs hire for service delivery and ignore leadership. "We'll add management later when we grow." Leaders try to do everything. Then they leave. Then you have nothing.

+

The Prevention

  • Right-size leadership BEFORE launch
  • Hire enough people who can actually move things forward
  • Distribute decision-making authority widely
  • Staff for leadership, not just service delivery
  • Build leadership depth. Never create a single point of failure.

9.9 Lessons Learned from the Empower Pilot

We are going to be honest about what we got wrong. These are not theoretical warnings. These are mistakes we made, lessons we earned, and changes that shaped everything we now recommend.

Data Capture is Critical from Day One

The Lesson

Get the wrong data from the start and your pilot will confuse everyone. You will not be able to prove what worked. You will not get more funding. This is a program killer.

What We Got Wrong

  • We did not define outcome metrics before launch
  • Our control group design was muddled. Support payments and coaching were not cleanly separated.
  • We treated data infrastructure as an afterthought instead of a foundation

What You Must Do

  • Define outcome metrics BEFORE you launch. Not six months in when you realize you measured the wrong things.
  • Design control groups with intention. Keep support payments equal. Isolate navigator coaching. Otherwise you cannot measure what the navigator actually does.
  • Build data infrastructure first. This is foundational. This is why we built our own navigator data tool.
  • Bring a data expert in during the design phase. Not after.

Funding Cannot Be an Afterthought

The Hard Truth

Empower faces staff layoffs because funding was not secured in advance. A program with proof of concept and real outcomes is cutting people because sustainability was not planned from the start. This is tragic. It is also preventable.

What Not to Do

  • Treat funding as "Phase 3" when you already have proof
  • Depend on a single funding stream
  • Wait until the grant runs out to start fundraising

What You Must Do

  • Build sustainability planning into Phase 1. Design outcomes and design funding at the same time.
  • Diversify funding from day one. Government + foundation + local/private.
  • Start building funder relationships the same day you launch the program
  • Never let funding runway drop below 12 months. Ever.

Leadership Alignment Takes Constant Work

The Reality Check

A brilliant shared vision statement means nothing if you do not reinforce it constantly. Without regular alignment, people drift back to their own interpretations and incentives. Every time.

Your Action Plan

  • Quarterly re-alignment sessions. Not status updates. Real conversations about whether decisions match the vision.
  • Shared vision requires ongoing reinforcement. One-time agreement is not enough.
  • Tie every major decision back to the vision statement. Out loud.
  • When new leadership joins, vision immersion is non-negotiable.

Navigator Caseloads Creep Up

The Silent Killer

Without monitoring, caseloads creep past effective ratios. It is gradual. One more family here. A partner referral there. Nobody notices until navigators are drowning and outcomes are tanking.

How to Prevent It

  • Build caseload caps into policy. Not guidelines. Policy.
  • Monitor actual caseloads monthly. Not quarterly.
  • When caseloads hit 85% of maximum, freeze new enrollment until you hire
  • Make caseload targets part of navigator performance metrics

Partner Coordination Requires Infrastructure

Handshakes Break Under Pressure

Informal partnerships collapse under stress. Great relationships at launch mean nothing when budgets tighten or leadership changes. What worked during the honeymoon phase fails when it matters most.

Build This Now

  • MOUs are non-negotiable. Not bureaucratic busywork. Protection for families.
  • Weekly coordination meetings. Not "as-needed." Weekly.
  • Shared data protocols so information flows reliably
  • Named point person at every partner organization

The Control Group Challenge

Theory vs. Reality

The plan: keep support payments equal, isolate navigator coaching as the variable. Sounds clean. Reality was messier than anyone expected.

What Happened

The control group eroded. Some control group families got ad hoc navigator help. Some families in the treatment group disengaged. Pristine separation in a real community is nearly impossible to maintain.

Our Workaround

  • Create categories based on actual navigator session frequency
  • Correlate session intensity with outcomes
  • Accept natural variation instead of fighting it
  • This gives you the data you need without unrealistic control group purity

Critical Lesson

Design your evaluation methodology with a data expert from the start. Do not figure this out mid-pilot. Get research and evaluation expertise in the room when you are designing Phase 1. Not after. Not later. Now.

Early Warning Signs

These are the signals that predict failure before it arrives. If you see 3 or more, stop everything and intervene:

Early Warning Checklist

□

Attendance dropping at program activities or coordination meetings

□

Staff leaving faster (especially key people in year 1)

□

Caseloads creeping above 1:25 and nobody is raising the alarm

□

Supervision meetings getting cancelled or becoming irregular

□

Data collection getting sloppy or incomplete

□

Partners skipping coordination meetings or sending someone who cannot make decisions

□

Funding panic dominating strategy conversations

□

Staff saying "that's not how we do it anymore" about core model elements

Programs That Turned It Around

Not every struggling program dies. Here is what it looks like when leaders catch the drift and fight back:

Alcohol and Drug Council of Middle Tennessee

The crisis: Program quality declining. Staff morale tanking. Years of organizational drift under previous leadership.

The turnaround:

  • New director sat down with every single staff member, one on one
  • Adopted an explicit "no mission-drift" funding policy. Turned down grants that would compromise the model.
  • Cut programs that did not fit the core mission. No sentimentality.
  • Created visible quick wins (renovated the meeting room in 2 months instead of a year of committee debate)

Result: The organization survived, thrived, and became more mission-focused than it had ever been.

Housing First Programs (National Scale)

The challenge: Funders required psychiatric assessment before housing. That contradicted the core Housing First principle: housing first, services second.

The solution:

  • Bent the rule: shifted psychiatric assessment from first contact to after engagement. Met compliance. Protected family choice.
  • Daily team meetings reinforced Housing First philosophy
  • Mobile supervision and real-time team support

Result: Maintained 3.62/4 fidelity scores across diverse sites while meeting funder requirements. Source: PMC Housing First Fidelity Research

The Common Thread

Programs that turn around share three traits: (1) Leaders willing to make hard decisions. (2) Explicit policies that protect the model over chasing funding. (3) Relentless investment in fidelity monitoring and staff support. They do not drift. They course-correct.

The Bottom Line: Why Programs Really Die

Good intentions do not save programs. Programs die from:

Structural Misalignment

Different rules, eligibility requirements, and funding streams pulling partners in opposite directions

Perverse Incentives

Budgets and metrics that punish collaboration and reward silos

Fidelity Neglect

Nobody checks whether the model is actually being followed. Only 3.5% of programs do.

Staff Instability

$54K per departure. 19% annual turnover. When staff leave, everything else breaks.

Programs that survive do three things: Monitor fidelity obsessively, weekly not annually. Protect the model over chasing money. Invest in staff retention like it is mission-critical infrastructure. Because it is.

The Pattern Behind the Patterns

Every killer in this chapter has one thing in common: it is invisible until it is almost too late. Programs do not die from one catastrophic failure. They die from accumulated drift.

The fix is not heroic intervention. It is:

  • Regular checks against each pattern in this chapter
  • Honest assessment. No sugarcoating.
  • Willingness to course-correct early, before it is comfortable
  • Leadership that watches for these signs like their program depends on it

Use this chapter as a quarterly diagnostic. Are any of these patterns showing up? Fix them now. Not next quarter. Now.
