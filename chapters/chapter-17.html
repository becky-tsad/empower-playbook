Scaling with Fidelity

Why most programs fail at scale, how the Nurse-Family Partnership became the gold standard, and the Empower Fidelity Framework for maintaining effectiveness while growing.

Evidence-based programs don't fail because they stop working. They fail because as they scale, they drift from the core elements that made them work in the first place. This chapter draws on implementation science research and the Nurse-Family Partnership's 45-year track record to build Empower's framework for scaling without losing effectiveness.

The Fidelity Paradox

Programs that allow unlimited local adaptation lose effectiveness. Programs that allow no adaptation fail to fit local contexts. The solution is defining what's non-negotiable (Core Model Elements) vs. what's adaptable (local implementation choices).

Source: Durlak & DuPre (2008), "Implementation Matters: A Review of Research on the Influence of Implementation on Program Outcomes"

17.1 Why Programs Fail at Scale

The research is sobering: most evidence-based programs lose their effectiveness when they scale beyond the original pilot site. Understanding why this happens is the first step to preventing it.

The Scale-Up Problem by the Numbers

52%

of nonprofits modify programs to fit available funding

Source: Nonprofit Finance Fund Survey, 2018

40-60%

reduction in effect sizes in real-world vs. controlled settings

Source: Weisz et al., "Promoting and Protecting Youth Mental Health"

Five Root Causes of Program Drift

Why Scaling Fails

1

Mission Drift from Funding Pressure

Programs modify their model to match what funders will pay for, rather than what works. "We'll just skip the home visits to fit the budget" becomes acceptable.

2

Staff Turnover Creates Knowledge Loss

When experienced staff leave and institutional knowledge isn't documented, each new hire makes different decisions. The "why" behind practices gets lost.

3

Lack of Standardization

Without a written operations guide, each site implements based on interpretation. "What we think the model is" diverges from "what the model actually is."

4

Quality Reduction Under Resource Constraints

Caseloads creep from 1:25 to 1:40 to 1:60. Visit frequency drops. Training shortens. Each change seems minor; cumulative effect is catastrophic.

5

Incremental "Improvements" Without Testing

Local sites make small changes they believe are improvements. Without evaluation, harmful modifications appear helpful until outcomes collapse.

"The gap between knowledge about effective programs and knowledge about how to implement them successfully is perhaps the most significant issue facing prevention science."
— Fixsen et al., Implementation Research: A Synthesis of the Literature (2005)

17.2 The NFP Model - Gold Standard for Scaling

The Nurse-Family Partnership (NFP) is one of the few social programs that has successfully scaled while maintaining—and even improving—effectiveness. With 45 years of implementation research and over 300,000 families served annually, NFP offers critical lessons for any program seeking to scale with fidelity.

The NFP Results That Matter

$8,860

Cost per family AT SCALE

$12,398

Cost per family in original RCTs

Key insight: NFP achieved LOWER costs at scale while maintaining outcomes—the opposite of typical program decline.

Source: Miller (2015), "Projected Outcomes of Nurse-Family Partnership Home Visitation"

17.3 Empower's Fidelity Framework

Drawing on NFP's approach and implementation science research, we've developed Empower's Fidelity Framework. This distinguishes between Core Model Elements (non-negotiable) and Adaptable Elements (local choice).

Core Model Elements (Non-Negotiable)

These eight elements are the "active ingredients" that produce outcomes. Remove or weaken any of them and the model loses effectiveness.

Empower Core Model Elements

1

Benefits Cliff Assessment Before Employment Planning

Every participant receives a comprehensive benefits cliff analysis BEFORE any employment or advancement planning begins. This is foundational—not optional.

2

Single Navigator Relationship for 18-24 Months Minimum

The same navigator stays with the participant throughout the entire journey. No handoffs between staff, no reassignments for convenience.

3

1:25 or Lower Caseload Ratio for Intensive Phase

During the first 12 months, navigators cannot carry more than 25 intensive cases. This enables the relationship depth that produces outcomes.

4

Wraparound Coordination with Documented Partner MOUs

Formal agreements with partner organizations define referral pathways, data sharing, and coordination protocols. Informal "relationships" aren't sufficient.

5

Real-Time Data Dashboard Tracking Outcomes

Not annual reports—real-time. Leaders and navigators must see current outcomes weekly. This enables rapid course correction.

6

225% FPL / ALICE Threshold as Self-Sufficiency Target

Exit criteria is defined by a measurable threshold, not arbitrary timelines. Participants graduate when they reach economic stability, not when funding runs out.

7

Employer Certification with Cliff Awareness Training

Employers who hire participants must complete Poverty IQ training. This ensures the receiving workplace supports—rather than undermines—participant progress.

8

Monthly Case Review with Supervisor

Every active case is reviewed monthly with a supervisor. This prevents navigator burnout, catches drift early, and ensures consistent quality.

Adaptable Elements (Local Choice)

These elements can and should be adapted to local context, population, and resources—without compromising fidelity.

Adaptable Elements

Specific Benefits Tracked

Varies by state—some states have more robust SNAP, others have better childcare subsidies. Adapt the cliff analysis to local benefit landscape.

Partner Organizations

Every community has different organizations. The requirement is formal MOUs; which partners is local choice.

Soft Skills Curriculum Content

The specific curriculum can be tailored to population (youth vs. adults, recovery vs. re-entry) while maintaining the requirement for soft skills development.

Timeline Beyond 18 Months

18 months is the minimum; programs can extend to 24, 36, or longer based on population needs and funding.

Primary Outcome Emphasis

Employment, graduation, housing stability, recovery milestones—the primary outcome varies by program focus.

Communication Methods

Text, app, phone, in-person meetings—whatever works for the population, as long as contact frequency is maintained.

17.5 Quality Assurance Infrastructure

Fidelity doesn't happen by accident. It requires deliberate infrastructure for training, supervision, monitoring, and continuous improvement.

Navigator Certification Pathway

  1. 1

    Pre-Service Training (40 hours)

    Before carrying any cases

  2. 2

    Supervised Practice (3 months)

    Reduced caseload with intensive supervision

  3. 3

    Competency Assessment

    Observed skills demonstration

  4. 4

    Certification

    Annual renewal with continuing education

Training Curriculum Requirements

  • Benefits cliff modeling (8 hours)
  • Motivational interviewing (16 hours)
  • Trauma-informed practice (8 hours)
  • Local benefits landscape (4 hours)
  • Data system proficiency (4 hours)

Supervision Protocols

  • Weekly team meetings (group supervision)
  • Monthly individual supervision (1:1)
  • Quarterly field observation
  • Annual performance review with competency assessment

Data Systems for Monitoring

  • Real-time outcome dashboard
  • Caseload monitoring alerts
  • Contact frequency tracking
  • Referral completion rates
  • Fidelity metrics by site

The NFP Lesson: Coaching Paired with Feedback

NFP research shows that training alone doesn't maintain fidelity. The combination of real-time performance data + coaching support is what prevents drift.

When supervisors see that a navigator's contact frequency is dropping, they can intervene immediately—before outcomes suffer. Data without coaching is just surveillance; coaching without data is just opinion.

Source: Fixsen et al. (2009), "Core Implementation Components"

17.6 Warning Signs of Model Drift

Drift rarely announces itself. It happens gradually, through small compromises that seem reasonable in the moment. Train supervisors and leaders to recognize these warning signs.

Early Warning Signs of Model Drift

Caseloads creeping above 1:30

"We'll just temporarily go to 32 cases until we hire someone." Temporary becomes permanent. The 1:25 ratio exists for a reason.

Benefits cliff assessment skipped "to save time"

"They just need a job, we can do the cliff analysis later." Later never comes. Participants hit cliffs that could have been avoided.

Navigator reassignments breaking relationships

"It's more efficient to have navigators specialize." Efficiency kills the relationship. The whole model depends on continuity.

Dashboard data not reviewed regularly

"We're too busy serving participants to look at data." You can't know if you're succeeding without looking. Flying blind leads to crashes.

Partner coordination meetings cancelled

"Everyone's too busy this month." One cancellation becomes the norm. Wraparound coordination degrades to referrals into a void.

Outcomes not measured or reported

"We know we're making a difference." Anecdotes replace evidence. Without measurement, you're just hoping.

Supervision sessions becoming "check-ins"

"Let's just do a quick update." Supervision without case review and skill coaching doesn't prevent drift—it enables it.

New staff "learning as they go"

"We'll train them on the job." Without structured pre-service training, each generation of staff drifts further from the model.

17.7 The Cost of Fidelity

Quality assurance infrastructure costs money. Training takes time. Supervision requires investment. Some leaders see fidelity as overhead to be minimized. This is a catastrophic mistake.

The Cost of Drift

  • X Outcomes decline — The program stops working, but nobody knows until it's too late
  • X Reputation damage — Funders and partners lose trust when results don't match promises
  • X Funding loss — Evidence-based claims become indefensible without fidelity
  • X Staff burnout — Without proper support, navigators burn out faster
  • X Participant harm — Families trust you with their futures; drift betrays that trust

The Return on Fidelity

  • + Sustained outcomes — The program keeps working as it scales
  • + NFP proves scale reduces cost — $8,860 at scale vs. $12,398 in trials
  • + Funder confidence — Evidence of fidelity attracts continued investment
  • + Staff retention — Good supervision and support reduces turnover
  • + Replication readiness — Documented fidelity enables expansion to new sites

The Bottom Line

Programs that drift lose effectiveness, then funding, then existence. Programs that maintain fidelity keep producing outcomes, attracting investment, and growing impact.

Fidelity isn't overhead. It's the reason the program works.

"The key issue is not whether to scale up evidence-based programs but how to scale them up without losing their essence. The alternative—scaling programs that have drifted into ineffectiveness—wastes resources and betrays the people the programs were designed to serve."
— Adapted from Horowitz et al., "Scaling Up Evidence-Based Practices"

Key Takeaway

Scaling with fidelity requires three commitments:

1 Define what's non-negotiable — The 8 Core Model Elements cannot bend
2 Build quality assurance infrastructure — Training, supervision, data, coaching
3 Monitor continuously — Watch for warning signs before outcomes collapse

The Nurse-Family Partnership proves it's possible. Empower's framework provides the blueprint.
