15.1 Benefits Cliff Research

The benefits cliff is real, measurable, and affecting millions of families. Here's how big the problem is -- and why cliff navigation isn't optional.

3+ Million

Households face benefits cliffs

The Atlanta Fed's CLIFF Dashboard shows over 3 million American families face benefits cliffs that punish them for earning more.

Source: Atlanta Fed CLIFF Dashboard

51%

Median effective marginal tax rate

Families with children on multiple benefits lose 51 cents of every additional dollar earned. The system literally taxes them for trying to get ahead.

Source: NBER Working Paper

95%+

Marginal tax rate when Medicaid is lost

The Medicaid cliff is the worst. When families cross income thresholds and lose coverage, they keep just 5 cents of every new dollar. Getting a raise becomes economic devastation.

Source: Kaiser Family Foundation

$5,991 - $6,000+

Childcare subsidy loss at cliff

Childcare subsidies create one of the steepest cliffs. A small raise can wipe out $6,000+ in annual childcare support. No rational person takes that deal.

Source: CLASP Research

Employer-Reported Impact

1 in 3 businesses

report employees turning down promotions, raises, or extra hours because of benefits cliff concerns.

Benefits cliffs don't just trap families. They strangle businesses trying to grow their workforce.

Source: Atlanta Fed Employer Survey

CLIFF Coaching Effectiveness

90%

Made action plans with CLIFF coaching

In the Atlanta Fed's pilot, 90% of families who got benefits cliff coaching built concrete action plans. Give people the map, and they move.

Key Tool: Atlanta Fed CLIFF Dashboard

The Atlanta Fed built the Career Ladder Identifier and Financial Forecaster (CLIFF) tools so families and navigators can see exactly where the cliffs are.

Access CLIFF Dashboard

15.2 Navigator Model Evidence

The navigator model isn't a nice idea. It's evidence-based practice with documented outcomes. EMPath and other intensive coaching programs prove what works -- and what doesn't -- in relationship-based support.

Recommended Caseload Ratios

1:25

Ratio for intensive work

EMPath's research is clear: navigators working with ALICE families need caseloads around 1:25. Go higher, and the intensity that drives results disappears.

1:15

Or lower for complex cases

For families facing multiple barriers -- recovery, re-entry, complex trauma -- the research says 1:15 or lower. More barriers demand more support.

Source: EMPath Research Publications

Critical Finding: Relationship Duration

< 6 Months

Produces negative outcomes

Relationships under 6 months can produce worse outcomes than no intervention at all. Ending the relationship before progress takes hold does real harm.

18-24 Months

Minimum for mobility impact

18-24 months is the minimum for real economic mobility. That's why Empower commits to staying with families for the long haul.

Source: EMPath Bridge to Self-Sufficiency Research

The Amplifying Effect of Trust

Trust amplifies everything. Every dollar invested in services produces better outcomes when delivered through a trusted navigator relationship versus transactional service delivery.

Why Trust Matters:

  • * Families share the full picture of what they're facing
  • * Follow-through on referrals jumps dramatically
  • * Crises get caught early -- before they become emergencies
  • * Goals get bigger and more realistic at the same time

EMPath Documented Outcomes

97%

Employment at exit

97% of families who complete EMPath's program are employed at exit. Intensive navigation works.

$32,548

Average income at exit

Families reach $32,548 average annual income at exit -- above the ALICE threshold for most areas.

Source: EMPath "What Works" Research

Why Urban-Designed Programs Fail in Rural Contexts

  • Transit: Urban programs assume buses exist. Rural families need vehicle solutions.
  • Service density: Urban programs assume services are nearby. Rural families drive 30+ miles for basics.
  • Anonymity: Urban programs assume privacy. In rural communities, everyone knows everyone.
  • Employer options: Urban programs assume job choices. Rural areas may have 1-2 major employers.
  • Technology: Urban programs assume internet. Rural broadband gaps are still massive.

15.4 Two-Generation Evidence

The two-generation approach -- addressing kids and parents at the same time -- produces better outcomes than serving either alone. The research is clear: families rise together or they don't rise at all.

CAP Tulsa CareerAdvance

Landmark two-generation study results

Parent certification rate

$3,000

Income increase = 17% child earnings boost

Source: CAP Tulsa CareerAdvance Research

15.6 Recovery & Economic Mobility

Recovery and economic mobility are inseparable. Employment during recovery produces better outcomes. Policy choices directly impact whether people stay clean or relapse.

Employment & Recovery: The Evidence

Employment During Treatment

Employment during treatment predicts better long-term recovery. Work provides structure, purpose, income, and connection -- all protective factors against relapse.

IPS Model: Most Evidence-Based

Individual Placement and Support (IPS) is the gold standard: rapid job placement with ongoing support. Place first, then train on the job. Not the other way around.

Source: IPS Employment Center

Medicaid Expansion & Substance Use Treatment

Uninsured Rate for Opioid Hospitalizations

13.4%

Before expansion

2.9%

After expansion

Overdose Rates in Expansion States

6% Lower

Medicaid expansion states: 6% fewer overdose deaths. Coverage saves lives. Full stop.

Source: Kaiser Family Foundation

Why This Matters for Empower

Many people in recovery depend on Medicaid, SNAP, and other benefits. Losing healthcare coverage during early recovery can trigger relapse. Empower's cliff navigation protects families during the exact transition point where the stakes are highest.

15.7 Policy Innovations That Work

Several states have found ways to smooth the cliffs. These policies work. They show what's possible when lawmakers actually understand the problem.

DC

DC Career MAP

District of Columbia

5-Year Transitional Assistance

Career MAP gives families 5 years of transitional TANF assistance. Benefits phase out gradually -- not cliff-style. Families can earn more without losing everything overnight.

Learn more
ME

Maine STEP Bill

State of Maine

75% Earned Income Disregard

Maine's STEP bill lets families keep 75% of earned income without it touching their benefit calculations. The cliff doesn't disappear, but it gets a lot less steep.

Learn more
CO

Colorado Works

State of Colorado

100% Earned Income Disregard

Colorado disregards 100% of earned income in the first months of employment. Starting a new job never triggers immediate benefit loss.

Learn more
MA

Massachusetts DTA

Commonwealth of Massachusetts

6-Month 100% Disregard

Massachusetts gives TAFDC families 6 months of 100% earned income disregard when they start working. A real bridge to let families stabilize.

Learn more

Using This Evidence for Policy Advocacy

These states prove cliff-smoothing works. When you advocate for policy changes in your state:

  • * Point to states that already did it successfully
  • * Connect policy changes to workforce outcomes employers care about
  • * Emphasize these are state-level decisions using existing flexibility
  • * Partner with employer associations who get the business impact

15.8 Methodology & Limitations

We're going to be straight about what we know, what we don't know, and what we're doing to close the gap. Transparency builds credibility. Overpromising destroys it.

Understanding Evidence Quality

Not all evidence is equal. Here's where the research in this playbook falls on the quality scale:

1

RCTs & Quasi-Experimental Studies

Benefits cliff research (Atlanta Fed), IPS employment model, some CAP Tulsa findings. Strongest evidence.

2

Longitudinal Observational Studies

EMPath outcomes, ALICE data, employer survey research. Good evidence, but no control groups.

3

Program-Level Outcome Data

Empower Upper Cumberland pilot results. Promising but preliminary; requires independent validation.

4

Expert Consensus & Practice Wisdom

Some implementation guidance, caseload recommendations. Useful for practice, but not proof of effectiveness.

Acknowledged Limitations

Here's what we don't have yet:

  • No Randomized Control Trial: Empower outcomes haven't been validated through an RCT yet. Current results are pre-post comparisons without a control group.
  • Selection Effects: Families who complete the program may be systematically different from those who don't. Motivated families might have achieved similar outcomes on their own.
  • Net Resources Metric Needed: Current metrics focus on wage gains. The better measure is "net resources" (income + remaining benefits - new costs). That tells you if families are actually better off.
  • Limited Longitudinal Data: We know what happens at exit. We don't yet know if gains hold 2-3 years later.
  • ROI Calculations: The 7.1:1 ROI figure is modeled, not measured. It assumes reduced benefit utilization, but actual savings haven't been directly tracked.
  • Generalizability: Upper Cumberland results may not transfer to every rural region. Different economies, demographics, and service landscapes matter.

Building Stronger Evidence: Our Roadmap

We're building toward rigorous proof. Here's the plan:

Independent Evaluation

Partnering with university researchers for third-party outcome evaluation.

Comparison Group Design

Working with state agencies to build matched comparison groups from administrative data.

Net Resources Tracking

Tracking the full picture: income, benefits, and costs. The only way to measure if families are truly better off.

Longitudinal Follow-Up

Following families at 12, 24, and 36 months after exit. Do the gains stick?

Peer Review

Getting our findings published in peer-reviewed journals and pressure-tested by outside experts.

Multi-Site Replication

As we scale, tracking outcomes across different sites to test whether results hold everywhere.

Guidance: Using This Evidence Responsibly

DO:

Cite evidence for specific claims. "Benefits cliffs affect 3+ million families" is well-documented. Use it.

DO:

Say Empower synthesizes evidence-based practices. The individual components are proven. The specific combination hasn't been RCT-validated yet.

DO:

Call pilot outcomes "promising preliminary results." Not "proven effectiveness."

DON'T:

Call the 7.1:1 ROI "proven." It's a modeled estimate. It needs validation.

DON'T:

Confuse "evidence-informed" with "rigorously proven." They're different. Credibility depends on knowing the difference.
