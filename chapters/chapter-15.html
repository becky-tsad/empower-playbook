15.1 Benefits Cliff Research

The benefits cliff is not a theoretical concept—it's a documented, measurable phenomenon affecting millions of American families. The research below quantifies the scale of the problem and demonstrates why cliff navigation is non-negotiable.

3+ Million

Households face benefits cliffs

The Atlanta Fed's CLIFF Dashboard estimates over 3 million American households face significant benefits cliffs that create disincentives to work or advance.

Source: Atlanta Fed CLIFF Dashboard

51%

Median effective marginal tax rate

For families with children receiving multiple benefits, research shows a median effective marginal tax rate of 51%—meaning families lose 51 cents of every additional dollar earned.

Source: NBER Working Paper

95%+

Marginal tax rate when Medicaid is lost

The Medicaid cliff is particularly severe. When families cross income thresholds and lose coverage, the effective marginal tax rate can exceed 95%, making advancement economically devastating.

Source: Kaiser Family Foundation

$5,991 - $6,000+

Childcare subsidy loss at cliff

Childcare subsidies represent one of the steepest cliffs. Families can lose $6,000+ in annual childcare support with a small income increase, making work advancement economically irrational.

Source: CLASP Research

Employer-Reported Impact

1 in 3 businesses

report employees declining promotions, raises, or additional hours due to benefits cliff concerns.

This finding from employer surveys demonstrates that benefits cliffs don't just affect families—they constrain business operations and workforce development.

Source: Atlanta Fed Employer Survey

CLIFF Coaching Effectiveness

90%

Made action plans with CLIFF coaching

In the Atlanta Fed's pilot program, 90% of participants who received benefits cliff coaching developed concrete action plans for navigating their cliff.

Key Tool: Atlanta Fed CLIFF Dashboard

The Federal Reserve Bank of Atlanta developed the Career Ladder Identifier and Financial Forecaster (CLIFF) tools to help families and case managers visualize benefits cliffs.

Access CLIFF Dashboard

15.2 Navigator Model Evidence

The navigator model isn't just a nice idea—it's evidence-based practice with documented outcomes. Research from EMPath and other intensive coaching programs demonstrates what works and what doesn't in relationship-based support.

Recommended Caseload Ratios

1:25

Ratio for intensive work

EMPath research indicates that navigators working with ALICE populations need caseloads of approximately 1:25 to provide the intensive support required for economic mobility.

1:15

Or lower for complex cases

For populations with multiple barriers (recovery, re-entry, complex trauma), research suggests ratios of 1:15 or lower for effective support.

Source: EMPath Research Publications

Critical Finding: Relationship Duration

< 6 Months

Produces negative outcomes

Research shows that relationships of less than 6 months can actually produce worse outcomes than no intervention—the disruption of ending the relationship before progress is made can be harmful.

18-24 Months

Minimum for mobility impact

Sustained relationships of 18-24 months are the minimum threshold for achieving meaningful economic mobility outcomes. This is why Empower commits to long-term engagement.

Source: EMPath Bridge to Self-Sufficiency Research

The Amplifying Effect of Trust

Research documents that trust-based relationships between navigators and participants produce an "amplifying effect"—every dollar invested in services produces better outcomes when delivered through trusted relationships versus transactional service delivery.

Why Trust Matters:

  • * Participants share more complete information about barriers
  • * Follow-through on referrals increases dramatically
  • * Crisis intervention happens earlier (before emergencies)
  • * Goal-setting becomes more ambitious and realistic

EMPath Documented Outcomes

97%

Employment at exit

97% of EMPath participants who complete the program are employed at exit—demonstrating that intensive navigation produces employment outcomes.

$32,548

Average income at exit

Participants achieve average annual income of $32,548 at program exit—above the ALICE threshold for most areas.

Source: EMPath "What Works" Research

Why Urban-Designed Programs Fail in Rural Contexts

  • Transit assumptions: Urban programs assume public transportation exists; rural workers need vehicle solutions
  • Service density: Urban programs assume services are nearby; rural families travel 30+ miles for basics
  • Anonymity assumptions: Urban programs assume client privacy; in rural areas, everyone knows everyone
  • Employer diversity: Urban programs assume job options; rural areas may have 1-2 major employers
  • Technology access: Urban programs assume internet; rural broadband gaps remain significant

15.6 Recovery & Economic Mobility

Substance use disorder recovery and economic mobility are deeply interconnected. Research shows that addressing employment during recovery produces better outcomes—and that policy choices significantly impact recovery success rates.

Employment & Recovery: The Evidence

Employment During Treatment

Research consistently shows that employment during treatment predicts better long-term recovery outcomes. Work provides structure, purpose, income, and social connection—all protective factors against relapse.

IPS Model: Most Evidence-Based

Individual Placement and Support (IPS) is the most evidence-based model for employment in recovery, emphasizing rapid job placement with ongoing support rather than "train then place."

Source: IPS Employment Center

Medicaid Expansion & Substance Use Treatment

Uninsured Rate for Opioid Hospitalizations

13.4%

Before expansion

2.9%

After expansion

Overdose Rates in Expansion States

6% Lower

States that expanded Medicaid saw 6% lower overdose death rates compared to non-expansion states.

Source: Kaiser Family Foundation

Why This Matters for Empower

Many individuals in recovery receive Medicaid, SNAP, and other benefits. Benefits cliff navigation is critical during the transition from treatment to employment—losing healthcare coverage during early recovery can be catastrophic. The Empower model's cliff navigation protects this vulnerable population during economic advancement.

15.7 Policy Innovations That Work

Several states have implemented policy innovations that address benefits cliffs and support economic mobility. These proven approaches demonstrate what's possible when policymakers understand the cliff problem.

DC Career MAP

District of Columbia

5-Year Transitional Assistance

Career MAP provides 5 years of transitional TANF assistance, allowing families to increase earnings gradually while maintaining support. Benefits phase out slowly rather than cliff-style.

Maine's STEP (Strengthening Transitional Economic Prosperity) bill allows families to keep 75% of earned income without affecting benefit calculations, dramatically reducing cliff severity.

Massachusetts provides a 6-month period of 100% earned income disregard for TAFDC recipients who begin employment, providing a bridge period for families to stabilize.

Using This Evidence for Policy Advocacy

These state examples demonstrate that cliff-smoothing policies are viable, implementable, and effective. When advocating for policy changes in your state:

  • * Reference states that have successfully implemented similar approaches
  • * Connect policy changes to workforce outcomes employers care about
  • * Emphasize that these are state-level decisions using existing flexibility
  • * Partner with employer associations who understand the business impact

15.8 Methodology & Limitations

Transparency about methodology is essential for credible evidence claims. This section documents the current state of Empower-specific evidence, acknowledges limitations, and outlines the path to stronger evaluation.

Understanding Evidence Quality

Not all evidence is equal. Here's how the evidence cited in this playbook maps to standard hierarchies:

1

RCTs & Quasi-Experimental Studies

Benefits cliff research (Atlanta Fed), IPS employment model, some CAP Tulsa findings. Strongest evidence.

2

Longitudinal Observational Studies

EMPath outcomes, ALICE data, employer survey research. Good evidence, but no control groups.

3

Program-Level Outcome Data

Empower Upper Cumberland pilot results. Promising but preliminary; requires independent validation.

4

Expert Consensus & Practice Wisdom

Some implementation guidance, caseload recommendations. Useful for practice, but not proof of effectiveness.

Acknowledged Limitations

In the interest of transparency, we acknowledge the following limitations in the current Empower evidence base:

  • No Randomized Control Trial: Empower-specific outcomes have not been validated through an RCT. Current results are based on pre-post comparisons without a formal control group.
  • Selection Effects: Participants who complete the program may differ systematically from those who don't—motivated participants may have achieved similar outcomes without intervention.
  • Net Resources Metric Needed: Current outcome metrics focus on wage gains. A more rigorous measure would be "net resources" (income + remaining benefits - new costs), which captures whether families are truly better off.
  • Limited Longitudinal Data: We have data on outcomes at program exit but limited data on whether gains are sustained 2-3 years post-exit.
  • ROI Calculations: The 7.1:1 ROI figure is based on modeled assumptions about reduced government benefit utilization; actual benefit savings have not been directly measured.
  • Generalizability: Results from Upper Cumberland may not generalize to other rural regions with different economic conditions, demographics, or service landscapes.

Building Stronger Evidence: Our Roadmap

We're committed to building an evidence base that meets rigorous standards. Here's what we're pursuing:

Independent Evaluation

Partnering with university researchers to conduct third-party evaluation of outcomes.

Comparison Group Design

Working with state agencies to establish matched comparison groups from administrative data.

Net Resources Tracking

Implementing comprehensive tracking of income, benefits, and costs to measure true financial improvement.

Longitudinal Follow-Up

Establishing protocols for 12, 24, and 36-month post-exit outcome tracking.

Peer Review

Submitting findings to peer-reviewed journals and presenting at academic conferences.

Multi-Site Replication

As the model scales, tracking outcomes across diverse sites to test generalizability.

Guidance: Using This Evidence Responsibly

DO:

Cite the evidence for specific claims (e.g., "Benefits cliffs affect 3+ million households" is well-documented).

DO:

Acknowledge that the Empower model synthesizes evidence-based practices, even if the specific combination hasn't been RCT-validated.

DO:

Present pilot outcomes as "promising preliminary results" rather than "proven effectiveness."

DON'T:

Claim the 7.1:1 ROI as definitively proven—it's a modeled estimate that requires validation.

DON'T:

Overstate claims by confusing "evidence-informed" with "rigorously proven."
